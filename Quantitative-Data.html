Chapter 1 - Arithmetic & Number System

1. Basic Operations & BODMAS

The basic operations in mathematics are fundamental processes that form the foundation for most mathematical calculations. These operations include:

1. Addition (+):

The process of combining two or more numbers to get a larger number.

Example:
5 + 3 = 8

2. Subtraction (−):

The process of removing one number from another.

Example:
7 − 4 = 3

3. Multiplication (×):

The process of adding a number to itself multiple times.

Example:
6 × 3 = 18

4. Division (÷):

The process of splitting a number into equal parts.

Example:
12 ÷ 4 = 3

BODMAS Rule

BODMAS stands for Brackets, Orders, Division and Multiplication (from left to right), Addition and Subtraction (from left to right). It is a set of rules that helps in determining the correct order of operations to simplify expressions and avoid confusion.

  • B - Brackets: First, solve expressions inside brackets (parentheses). Brackets include round brackets (), square brackets [], and curly brackets {}.

    • Example:
    (5 + 3) × 2 = 8 × 2 = 16

  • O - Orders: This includes powers or exponents, such as squares, cubes, square roots, etc.

    • Example:
    2³ = 8

  • D and M - Division and Multiplication: After brackets and orders, division and multiplication are performed from left to right. They have equal priority, so they are handled in the order they appear.

    • Example:
    10 ÷ 2 × 5 = 5 × 5 = 25

  • A and S - Addition and Subtraction: Finally, addition and subtraction are performed from left to right. These also have equal priority.

    • Example:
    8 + 2 − 3 = 10 − 3 = 7

Example of BODMAS in Action:

Simplify the following expression:

6 + (5 × 2) − 4 ÷ 2

Step 1: Brackets first
6 + 10 − 4 ÷ 2

Step 2: Division
6 + 10 − 2

Step 3: Addition and Subtraction (from left to right)
16 − 2 = 14

So, the result of the expression is 14.

Following BODMAS ensures that we always perform calculations in the correct order and get the correct results.

2. Factors, Multiples, and Divisibility

Factors

  • A factor of a number is any number that divides that number exactly, without leaving a remainder.
  • Example: Factors of 12: 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 12
  • How to Find Factors:
    • Start with 1 and the number itself.
    • Check for other numbers that divide the given number exactly (i.e., no remainder).
    • List all such numbers.

Multiples

  • A multiple of a number is the product of that number and any integer.
  • Example: Multiples of 5: 5, 10, 15, 20, 25, 30, ...
  • How to Find Multiples:
    • Start with the number itself.
    • Multiply that number by different integers (1, 2, 3, 4, etc.).
    • Continue the process to get an infinite list of multiples.

Divisibility

  • A number is divisible by another number if, when divided, there is no remainder.
  • Common Divisibility Rules:
    • Divisibility by 2: A number is divisible by 2 if its last digit is even (0, 2, 4, 6, 8).
    • Divisibility by 3: A number is divisible by 3 if the sum of its digits is divisible by 3.
    • Divisibility by 4: A number is divisible by 4 if the last two digits form a number divisible by 4.
    • Divisibility by 5: A number is divisible by 5 if its last digit is 0 or 5.
    • Divisibility by 6: A number is divisible by 6 if it is divisible by both 2 and 3.
    • Divisibility by 9: A number is divisible by 9 if the sum of its digits is divisible by 9.

Relation Between Factors and Multiples

  • Factors of a number are the numbers that divide it exactly.
  • Multiples of a number are the numbers you get when you multiply that number by integers.
  • Example:
    • Factors of 6: 1, 2, 3, 6
    • Multiples of 6: 6, 12, 18, 24, 30, ...

Summary

  • Factors: Numbers that divide a given number exactly (without a remainder).
  • Multiples: Numbers that are the product of a given number and any integer.
  • Divisibility: A rule that helps determine whether one number can be divided by another without a remainder.

3. LCM (Least Common Multiple) & HCF (Highest Common Factor)

LCM (Least Common Multiple)

  • The LCM of two or more numbers is the smallest number that is a multiple of all the given numbers.
  • It is the smallest common multiple shared by the numbers.
  • How to Find the LCM:
    • List the multiples of each number.
    • Find the smallest multiple that is common to all numbers.
    • Alternatively, the LCM of two numbers can also be found using the formula: LCM ( a , b ) = |a × b| HCF(a, b)
  • Example: Find the LCM of 4 and 6.
    • Multiples of 4: 4, 8, 12, 16, 20, 24, ...
    • Multiples of 6: 6, 12, 18, 24, 30, ...
    • The smallest common multiple is 12, so the LCM of 4 and 6 is 12.

HCF (Highest Common Factor)

  • The HCF of two or more numbers is the largest number that divides all the given numbers exactly, without leaving a remainder.
  • It is also known as the GCD (Greatest Common Divisor).
  • How to Find the HCF:
    • List the factors of each number.
    • Find the largest number that is common to all the factor lists.
    • Alternatively, the HCF of two numbers can also be found using the formula: HCF ( a , b ) = |a × b| LCM(a, b)
  • Example: Find the HCF of 12 and 18.
    • Factors of 12: 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 12
    • Factors of 18: 1, 2, 3, 6, 9, 18
    • The largest common factor is 6, so the HCF of 12 and 18 is 6.

Relation Between LCM and HCF

  • There is an important relationship between the LCM and HCF of two numbers: LCM ( a , b ) × HCF ( a , b ) = |a × b|
  • Example:
    • For 12 and 18, we already know:
    • LCM = 36
    • HCF = 6
    • Now, check the relationship:
      • LCM(12, 18) × HCF(12, 18) = 36 × 6 = 216
      • |12 × 18| = 12 × 18 = 216
    • Since both results are equal, this confirms the relationship between LCM and HCF.

4. Prime Numbers & Composite Numbers

Prime Numbers

  • A prime number is a natural number greater than 1 that has no positive divisors other than 1 and itself.
  • In simple terms, it can only be divided by 1 and the number itself, meaning it cannot be formed by multiplying two smaller natural numbers.

    • Characteristics of Prime Numbers:

    • Prime numbers are greater than 1.
    • They have only two factors: 1 and the number itself.

    Examples of Prime Numbers:

    • 2 (only divisible by 1 and 2)
    • 3 (only divisible by 1 and 3)
    • 5 (only divisible by 1 and 5)
    • 7 (only divisible by 1 and 7)
    • 11 (only divisible by 1 and 11)

    Notice that 2 is the only even prime number. All other even numbers are not prime because they are divisible by 2.

    How to Check if a Number is Prime:

    • Check if the number n is divisible by any smaller number (starting from 2) up to n.
    • If it is divisible by any number other than 1 and itself, it is not a prime number.

Composite Numbers

  • A composite number is a natural number greater than 1 that has more than two factors.
  • In other words, composite numbers can be divided exactly by numbers other than 1 and themselves. These numbers can be formed by multiplying two smaller natural numbers.

    • Characteristics of Composite Numbers:

    • Composite numbers are greater than 1.
    • They have more than two factors.

    Examples of Composite Numbers:

    • 4 (divisible by 1, 2, and 4)
    • 6 (divisible by 1, 2, 3, and 6)
    • 8 (divisible by 1, 2, 4, and 8)
    • 9 (divisible by 1, 3, and 9)
    • 12 (divisible by 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, and 12)

Difference Between Prime and Composite Numbers

Property Prime Numbers Composite Numbers
Divisors Exactly two divisors: 1 and itself More than two divisors
Examples 2, 3, 5, 7, 11, 13, 17 4, 6, 8, 9, 10, 12, 14
Even Prime Only 2 is even No even composite number is prime
Smallest Prime 2 No smallest composite (smallest is 4)

Special Case: Number 1

  • The number 1 is neither a prime nor a composite number. It only has one divisor (itself), so it doesn't meet the criteria for being prime or composite.

1. Linear Equations & Word Problems

Linear Equations

A linear equation is an equation of the form:

a x + b = 0

Where:

  • a and b are constants (numbers),
  • x is the variable (the unknown),
  • The equation represents a straight line when plotted on a graph.

A linear equation is called so because the variable x is raised to the first power (no exponents other than 1), and it graphs as a straight line.

Example of a Linear Equation:

Solve for x:

3 x - 7 = 11

Steps:

  • Add 7 to both sides: 3 x = 11 + 7 → 3 x = 18
  • Divide both sides by 3: x = 18 3 = 6

Thus, x = 6.

Word Problems Involving Linear Equations

Word problems are a practical way to apply linear equations to solve real-life situations. The key is to translate the problem into a mathematical equation and then solve it. Here are some steps to follow when solving word problems:

  • Understand the problem: Read the problem carefully to understand what is given and what needs to be found.
  • Assign variables: Let a variable represent the unknown quantity (e.g., x).
  • Set up the equation: Use the information in the problem to form a linear equation.
  • Solve the equation: Use algebraic methods to solve for the variable.
  • Check the solution: Verify that the solution makes sense in the context of the problem.

Example Word Problem 1:

Problem: The sum of a number and 8 is 15. What is the number?

Solution:

  • Let the unknown number be x.
  • Set up the equation: x + 8 = 15
  • Solve for x: Subtract 8 from both sides: x = 15 - 8 = 7

The number is 7.

Example Word Problem 2:

Problem: A car rental company charges a fixed fee of $20 per day plus $0.15 per mile driven. If a person rents a car for one day and drives 200 miles, how much will the total cost be?

Solution:

  • Let the total cost be C.
  • The cost for the day is the fixed fee of $20, and the cost per mile is $0.15 per mile for 200 miles. So, the cost for the miles driven is 0.15 × 200 = 30 .
  • Set up the equation for the total cost: C = 20 + 0.15 × 200
  • Simplify the equation: C = 20 + 30 = 50

The total cost is $50.

Example Word Problem 3:

Problem: Sarah is twice as old as Tom. In 5 years, Sarah will be 5 years older than Tom. How old are Sarah and Tom now?

Solution:

  • Let Tom's age be x.
  • Since Sarah is twice as old as Tom, Sarah’s age is 2x.
  • In 5 years, Tom's age will be x + 5, and Sarah's age will be 2x + 5.
  • According to the problem, in 5 years, Sarah will be 5 years older than Tom, so: 2 x + 5 = ( x + 5 ) + 5
  • Simplify the equation: 2 x + 5 = x + 10
  • Subtract x from both sides: x + 5 = 10
  • Subtract 5 from both sides: x = 5

Tom’s age is 5, and Sarah’s age is 2 × 5 = 10 .

So, Tom is 5 years old, and Sarah is 10 years old.

2. Quadratic Equations & Roots

Definition

  • A quadratic equation is a second-degree polynomial equation of the form: ax2 + bx + c = 0.
  • The solutions or roots of a quadratic equation are the values of x that satisfy the equation.

General Form of a Quadratic Equation

The general form of a quadratic equation is:

  • ax2 + bx + c = 0

Example

  • 2x2 + 3x − 5 = 0
  • Here, a = 2, b = 3, and c = −5.

Methods to Solve Quadratic Equations

  1. Factoring
    • Factoring is the process of writing the quadratic equation as a product of two binomials.
    • Example: Solve x2 + 5x + 6 = 0 by factoring.
    • Factor the equation as (x + 2)(x + 3) = 0 and solve for x:
      • x = -2 or x = -3
  2. Completing the Square
    • Completing the square is a method used to rewrite the quadratic equation in the form of a perfect square trinomial.
    • Example: Solve x2 + 6x − 7 = 0 by completing the square.
    • Move −7 to the other side, then add 9 to both sides to complete the square:
      • Factor the left side as (x + 3)2 = 16
      • Solve for x: x = −3 ± 4, so x = 1 or x = −7
  3. Quadratic Formula
    • The quadratic formula is a universal method to solve any quadratic equation. It is:
      • x = −b ± √(b2 − 4ac)} /2a
    • Example: Solve 2x2 + 3x − 5 = 0 using the quadratic formula.
      • Identify a = 2, b = 3, c = −5
      • Plug these values into the formula to get x = 1 and x = −2.5

Discriminant

The discriminant is the expression under the square root in the quadratic formula:

  • Δ = b2 − 4ac

The value of the discriminant determines the nature of the roots of the quadratic equation:

  • If Δ > 0, the equation has two real and distinct roots.
  • If Δ = 0, the equation has one real root (a repeated root).
  • If Δ < 0, the equation has two complex roots.

3. Inequalities & Their Applications

Definition

  • Inequalities are mathematical expressions that describe the relationship between two values where one is not necessarily equal to the other.
  • They are used extensively in fields such as economics, engineering, and everyday decision-making to represent constraints, limitations, and preferences.

Types of Inequalities

  • Less than (<):
    a < b means a is smaller than b.
  • Greater than (>):
    a > b means a is greater than b.
  • Less than or equal to (≤):
    a ≤ b means a is smaller than or equal to b.
  • Greater than or equal to (≥):
    a ≥ b means a is greater than or equal to b.
  • Not equal to (≠):
    a ≠ b means a is not equal to b.

Solving Inequalities

To solve an inequality, the goal is to find the range of values for the variable that makes the inequality true. The steps for solving inequalities are similar to solving equations, but there are a few key differences, especially when multiplying or dividing by a negative number.

Steps to Solve Linear Inequalities:

  1. Simplify the inequality (if necessary).
  2. Isolate the variable on one side by adding, subtracting, multiplying, or dividing both sides of the inequality.
  3. Reverse the inequality sign if multiplying or dividing both sides by a negative number.
  4. Graph the solution (if needed), or express it in interval notation.

Example 1:

Solve the inequality:

3x − 5 ≥ 7

  • Step 1: Add 5 to both sides:
    • 3x ≥ 12
  • Step 2: Divide both sides by 3:
    • x ≥ 4

    The solution is x ≥ 4.

Example 2:

Solve the inequality:

−2x + 4 < 10

  • Step 1: Subtract 4 from both sides:
    • −2x < 6
  • Step 2: Divide both sides by −2, and reverse the inequality:
    • x > −3

    The solution is x > −3.

Compound Inequalities

A compound inequality involves two inequalities combined by "and" or "or".

AND Inequality (Conjunction):

  • Both inequalities must be true at the same time.

Example of an AND Inequality:

Solve the compound inequality:

1 ≤ x + 3 ≤ 5

  • Step 1: Solve for x by isolating it in both parts of the inequality:
    • −2 ≤ x ≤ 2

    The solution is −2 ≤ x ≤ 2.

OR Inequality (Disjunction):

  • At least one of the inequalities must be true.

Example of an OR Inequality:

Solve the compound inequality:

x − 4 < −2 or x + 6 > 10

  • Step 1: Solve each inequality separately:
    • x < 2 or x > 4

    The solution is x < 2 or x > 4.

Graphing Inequalities

The solution to an inequality can often be represented graphically on a number line or coordinate plane.

  • For a linear inequality like x ≥ 4, the solution can be represented by a shaded region starting from 4 and extending infinitely to the right. The point 4 is included in the solution, represented by a closed circle.
  • For strict inequalities like x < 3, the solution is represented by a shaded region to the left of 3, and the point 3 is not included, represented by an open circle.

Example:

Solve x ≤ 2 and graph the solution.

The solution is all values of x less than or equal to 2.

On the number line, you would draw a closed circle at 2 and shade to the left.

Applications of Inequalities

Inequalities are used in various fields to model and solve problems where quantities are constrained by limits. Some common applications include:

  • Budgeting and Finance: Inequalities are used to represent constraints on spending. For example, a person might have a budget of $500, and they need to make sure their total spending doesn't exceed this amount.
  • Optimization Problems: Inequalities are used to represent constraints in optimization problems. For example, a company may need to maximize profit while respecting limits on resources such as manpower, raw materials, or time.
  • Engineering and Physics: Inequalities model physical limitations, such as maximum capacity or safe limits. For example, the stress on a beam must not exceed a certain threshold to ensure safety.
  • Population and Demographics: Inequalities can be used to model population growth, ensuring that the population size does not exceed a certain threshold.
  • Time Management: Inequalities can model time constraints, such as needing to complete a task within a set time.

4. Sequences & Series (AP, GP, HP)

Definition

  • A sequence is an ordered list of numbers.
  • A series is the sum of the terms of a sequence.
  • Three important types of sequences and series are:
    • Arithmetic Progression (AP)
    • Geometric Progression (GP)
    • Harmonic Progression (HP)

1. Arithmetic Progression (AP)

An Arithmetic Progression (AP) is a sequence of numbers where the difference between consecutive terms is constant. This difference is called the common difference d.

General Form of an AP

  • a, a+d, a+2d, a+3d, …
  • Where:
    • a is the first term.
    • d is the common difference between terms.

nth Term of an AP

  • The nth term Tₙ of an AP is given by the formula: Tₙ = a + (n - 1) * d
  • Where:
    • a is the first term.
    • d is the common difference.
    • n is the number of terms.

Sum of First n Terms of an AP

  • The sum Sₙ of the first n terms of an AP is given by: Sₙ = n/2 * [2a + (n - 1) * d]
  • Alternatively, if the last term l is known: Sₙ = n/2 * (a + l)

Example of AP:

  • Consider the sequence 2, 5, 8, 11, …:
    • a = 2, d = 3.
    • The nth term is Tₙ = 2 + (n - 1) * 3 = 3n - 1.
    • The sum of the first 5 terms is: S₅ = 5/2 * (2 + 14) = 40.

2. Geometric Progression (GP)

A Geometric Progression (GP) is a sequence of numbers where each term after the first is found by multiplying the previous term by a constant. This constant is called the common ratio r.

General Form of a GP

  • a, ar, ar², ar³, …
  • Where:
    • a is the first term.
    • r is the common ratio between terms.

nth Term of a GP

  • The nth term Tₙ of a GP is given by the formula: Tₙ = a * r^(n - 1)

Sum of First n Terms of a GP

  • The sum Sₙ of the first n terms of a GP is given by: Sₙ = a * (1 - rⁿ) / (1 - r) for r ≠ 1
  • If r = 1, the sum is simply: Sₙ = n * a

Example of GP:

  • Consider the sequence 3, 6, 12, 24, …:
    • a = 3, r = 2.
    • The nth term is Tₙ = 3 * 2^(n - 1).
    • The sum of the first 4 terms is: S₄ = 3 * (1 - 2⁴) / (1 - 2) = 45.

3. Harmonic Progression (HP)

A Harmonic Progression (HP) is a sequence where the reciprocals of the terms form an Arithmetic Progression (AP).

General Form of an HP

  • If the terms of the HP are a₁, a₂, a₃, …, then the reciprocals 1/a₁, 1/a₂, 1/a₃, … form an AP.

nth Term of an HP

  • The nth term of an HP can be expressed as: aₙ = 1 / Tₙ, where Tₙ is the corresponding term in the AP formed by the reciprocals.

Example of HP:

  • Consider the sequence 1, 1/2, 1/3, 1/4, …:
    • The reciprocals form the AP: 1, 2, 3, 4, ….
    • Hence, the common difference of the reciprocals is 1.

Key Differences Between AP, GP, and HP

  • Common Difference vs. Common Ratio:
    • AP has a common difference d.
    • GP has a common ratio r.
    • HP has the reciprocal of a common difference.
  • Behavior:
    • In AP, terms increase or decrease linearly.
    • In GP, terms increase or decrease exponentially.
    • In HP, terms decrease rapidly initially but then level out.

Applications of Sequences and Series

  • In Finance: AP is used in savings, loans, and amortizations. GP is used in compound interest and population models.
  • In Physics: GP is used in radioactive decay and exponential decay models.
  • In Computer Science: Sequences and series are used in algorithm analysis and performance evaluation.

5. Logarithms & Exponents

1. Exponents

  • An exponent represents the number of times a base number is multiplied by itself. It's written as an, where a is the base and n is the exponent.

Properties of Exponents

  • Product of Powers Rule: am ⋅ an = am+n
  • Quotient of Powers Rule: am / an = am-n
  • Power of a Power Rule: (am)n = am⋅n
  • Power of a Product Rule: (ab)n = an ⋅ bn
  • Power of a Quotient Rule: (a/b)n = an / bn
  • Zero Exponent Rule: a0 = 1 (for a ≠ 0)
  • Negative Exponent Rule: a-n = 1 / an
  • Fractional Exponent Rule: a1/n = n√a and am/n = n√(am)

Example:

  • 23 = 2 ⋅ 2 ⋅ 2 = 8
  • 52 / 56 = 5-2 = 1 / 52 = 625
  • (32)3 = 32 ⋅ 3 = 36 = 729

2. Logarithms

  • A logarithm is the inverse operation of exponentiation. It answers the question: "To what power must a given base be raised to produce a certain number?"

Logarithmic Form

  • The logarithmic form is written as: logb a = n, which means bn = a
  • b is the base of the logarithm, a is the argument (the number you're taking the logarithm of), and n is the logarithm or the power.

Common Logarithms

  • Base 10 Logarithm (Common Logarithm): log10 a, often written as log a.
  • Base e Logarithm (Natural Logarithm): loge a or ln a, where e ≈ 2.718.
  • Base 2 Logarithm: log2 a, commonly used in computer science.

Properties of Logarithms

  • Product Rule: logb(a ⋅ c) = logb a + logb c
  • Quotient Rule: logb(a / c) = logb a - logb c
  • Power Rule: logb an = n ⋅ logb a
  • Change of Base Rule: logb a = logc a / logc b (where c is any positive base)
  • Logarithm of 1: logb 1 = 0 (for any b > 0)
  • Logarithm of the Base: logb b = 1

Example:

  • log2 8 = 3 because 23 = 8
  • log10 1000 = 3 because 103 = 1000
  • ln e5 = 5 because e5 = e5

2. Profit, Loss & Discount

Definition

  • These concepts are widely used in business, commerce, and financial transactions. They deal with how much money is made or lost on a transaction, and how much a discount reduces the original price of an item.

i. Profit

  • Profit is the positive difference between the selling price and the cost price. When the selling price is greater than the cost price, the seller earns a profit.
  • Formula for Profit:

    Profit = Selling Price (SP) - Cost Price (CP)

  • Selling Price (SP): The price at which an item is sold.
  • Cost Price (CP): The price at which an item is bought or the cost to produce it.

Profit Percentage

  • To calculate profit percentage, use the formula:

    Profit Percentage = (Profit / Cost Price) × 100

  • Example: If the cost price of a product is $500, and it is sold for $600, the profit is:

    Profit = 600 - 500 = 100

  • The profit percentage is:

    Profit Percentage = (100 / 500) × 100 = 20%

ii. Loss

  • Loss occurs when the selling price is less than the cost price. In this case, the seller sells the product at a price lower than what was paid for it, resulting in a loss.
  • Formula for Loss:

    Loss = Cost Price (CP) - Selling Price (SP)

Loss Percentage

  • To calculate the loss percentage, use the formula:

    Loss Percentage = (Loss / Cost Price) × 100

  • Example: If the cost price of a product is $500, and it is sold for $400, the loss is:

    Loss = 500 - 400 = 100

  • The loss percentage is:

    Loss Percentage = (100 / 500) × 100 = 20%

iii. Discount

  • Discount is the reduction in the selling price of an item, usually given as a percentage of the original price. It is commonly offered during sales or promotions to encourage customers to buy.
  • Formula for Discount:

    Discount = (Discount Percentage / 100) × Marked Price (MP)

  • Marked Price (MP): The original price of the item before any discount is applied.
  • Discount Percentage: The percentage of the discount offered.

Selling Price After Discount

  • To find the selling price after the discount is applied, subtract the discount from the marked price:

    Selling Price (SP) = Marked Price (MP) - Discount

  • Example: If the marked price of a product is $1000 and a 20% discount is offered, the discount amount is:

    Discount = (20 / 100) × 1000 = 200

  • The selling price after discount is:

    Selling Price = 1000 - 200 = 800

iv. Profit or Loss on Discounted Price

  • In some cases, an item might be sold at a discount, but you want to know if there is still a profit or loss. This can happen when the cost price is different from the discounted selling price.
  • Formula for Profit or Loss with Discount:

    Profit or Loss = Selling Price after Discount - Cost Price

  • If the result is positive, it’s a profit. If the result is negative, it’s a loss.

Example

  • If the cost price of a product is $700, the marked price is $1000, and a 30% discount is offered, then:

    Discount = (30 / 100) × 1000 = 300

  • The selling price after discount is:

    Selling Price = 1000 - 300 = 700

  • The profit or loss is:

    Profit or Loss = 700 - 700 = 0

  • In this case, there is no profit or loss (break-even).

v. Applications of Profit, Loss, and Discount

  • Business Pricing: These concepts help businesses decide the price of goods and services, ensuring they make a profit or avoid selling at a loss.
  • Sales and Promotions: Discounts are used in sales strategies to attract customers and increase sales volumes.
  • Personal Finance: Understanding profit, loss, and discounts helps individuals make informed decisions when buying goods or services at discounted prices.
  • Market Analysis: Businesses use profit and loss calculations to assess the performance of their products or services in the market.

3. Simple Interest & Compound Interest

Definition

  • Interest is the cost of borrowing money or the return on investment for lending money. It is usually calculated as a percentage of the principal (the initial amount of money) over a specified period of time. The two main types of interest are Simple Interest and Compound Interest.

i. Simple Interest (SI)

  • Simple Interest is calculated only on the principal amount for the entire duration of the loan or investment. The interest does not accumulate on the previously earned interest.
  • Formula for Simple Interest:

    Simple Interest (SI) = (P × R × T) / 100

  • Where:
    • P = Principal amount (the initial amount of money)
    • R = Rate of interest per annum
    • T = Time period in years

Total Amount (A)

  • The Total Amount (A) is the sum of the principal and the interest:

    Total Amount (A) = P + Simple Interest

    or

    A = P + (P × R × T) / 100

Example

  • If you invest $1000 at an annual interest rate of 5% for 3 years, the simple interest would be:

    SI = (1000 × 5 × 3) / 100 = 150

  • The total amount after 3 years is:

    A = 1000 + 150 = 1150

ii. Compound Interest (CI)

  • Compound Interest is calculated on the principal amount as well as the accumulated interest from previous periods. It allows the interest to accumulate and grow over time, leading to a higher amount of interest compared to simple interest.
  • Formula for Compound Interest:

    Compound Interest (CI) = P × (1 + R / 100) ^ T - P

  • Where:
    • P = Principal amount
    • R = Rate of interest per annum
    • T = Time period in years

Total Amount (A)

  • The Total Amount (A) after interest is added is:

    A = P × (1 + R / 100) ^ T

Example

  • If you invest $1000 at an annual interest rate of 5% for 3 years, the compound interest would be:

    A = 1000 × (1 + 5 / 100) ^ 3 = 1000 × 1.157625 = 1157.63

  • The compound interest is:

    CI = 1157.63 - 1000 = 157.63

iii. Differences Between Simple Interest and Compound Interest

Feature Simple Interest (SI) Compound Interest (CI)
Interest Calculation Only on the principal amount On both principal and accumulated interest
Growth of Interest Linear growth Exponential growth

Total Amount A = P + SI A = P × (1 + R / 100) ^ T

iv. Applications of Simple and Compound Interest

  • Simple Interest:
    • Used in short-term loans (e.g., personal loans, car loans, etc.)
    • Applied to certain types of investments, like savings accounts that offer a fixed rate of return.
    • Generally used for calculating interest on low-risk, short-duration loans or deposits.
  • Compound Interest:
    • Used in long-term loans (e.g., mortgages, credit cards, and student loans).
    • Commonly used in investment scenarios like mutual funds, stocks, and retirement accounts where returns are reinvested.
    • It benefits from compounding, where interest is earned on the accumulated interest, leading to faster growth.

v. Compound Interest with Different Compounding Periods

  • Compound interest can be compounded at different intervals: annually, semi-annually, quarterly, monthly, etc. The formula is slightly modified for these cases.
  • Formula for Compound Interest with Different Periods:

    A = P × (1 + R / (n × 100)) ^ (n × T)

  • Where:
    • P = Principal amount
    • R = Annual interest rate
    • T = Time period in years
    • n = Number of compounding periods per year (e.g., for monthly compounding, n = 12).

Example with Monthly Compounding

  • If you invest $1000 at an annual interest rate of 6% for 2 years, with monthly compounding, the formula becomes:

    A = 1000 × (1 + 6 / (12 × 100)) ^ (12 × 2) = 1000 × (1.005) ^ 24 ≈ 1000 × 1.12749 = 1127.49

  • The compound interest is:

    CI = 1127.49 - 1000 = 127.49

4. Successive Percentage Change

Definition

  • Successive Percentage Change refers to a situation where two or more percentage changes occur in sequence, one after the other. The overall effect of successive percentage changes is different from simply adding or subtracting the individual percentage changes. Instead, the overall percentage change must be calculated using a compounded approach.

Formula for Successive Percentage Change

  • When you have two successive percentage changes, say an increase of p1% followed by an increase of p2%, the total percentage change can be calculated using the following formula:

    Overall Percentage Change = ((1 + p1 / 100) × (1 + p2 / 100) - 1) × 100

  • Where:
    • p1 = First percentage change
    • p2 = Second percentage change

General Formula for n Successive Percentage Changes

  • For n successive percentage changes, the formula is:

    Overall Percentage Change = ( ∏ (1 + pi / 100) - 1 ) × 100

  • Where pi represents the i-th percentage change.

Explanation with Example

Example 1: Two Successive Percentage Increases

  • Suppose a product's price increases by 10% in the first year and 15% in the second year. To find the overall percentage increase over the two years:
    • First Change: 10% increase

      1 + 10 / 100 = 1.10

    • Second Change: 15% increase

      1 + 15 / 100 = 1.15

  • Now, calculate the overall percentage change:

    Overall Percentage Change = (1.10 × 1.15 - 1) × 100 = (1.265 - 1) × 100 = 0.265 × 100 = 26.5%

  • So, the overall percentage increase is 26.5%.

Example 2: Successive Percentage Increase and Decrease

  • Now, consider a price increase of 20% followed by a price decrease of 10%. The formula works as follows:
    • First Change: 20% increase

      1 + 20 / 100 = 1.20

    • Second Change: 10% decrease

      1 - 10 / 100 = 0.90

  • Now calculate the overall percentage change:

    Overall Percentage Change = (1.20 × 0.90 - 1) × 100 = (1.08 - 1) × 100 = 0.08 × 100 = 8%

  • So, the overall percentage change is an 8% increase.

Why Adding Percentages Doesn’t Work

  • It might seem intuitive to just add the percentages (for example, 10% + 15% = 25%), but this approach doesn’t take into account the compounding effect, where the second change is applied to the new value after the first change. This is why successive percentage changes must be calculated using the formula rather than simply adding the percentages together.

Applications of Successive Percentage Change

  • Price Changes: Successive percentage changes are commonly used in retail pricing, where the price of a product might increase in stages due to factors like inflation, supply chain issues, or seasonal promotions.
  • Investment Growth: Compound interest in investments, where returns are generated on top of previous returns, involves successive percentage changes over time.
  • Taxation: When a new tax rate is applied to an amount already taxed, successive percentage changes are involved.
  • Discounts: Successive discounts can occur when a product is first offered at one discount, and then another discount is applied to the already reduced price.

5. Marked Price & Selling Price

Definition

  • In the context of business, retail, and pricing, Marked Price and Selling Price are two key terms used to describe the price of an item. Understanding the relationship between these prices is important for calculating discounts, profits, and losses.

i. Marked Price (MP)

  • The Marked Price is the original price that is listed or displayed on an item before any discount or reduction is applied. It is the price at which a seller initially offers a product for sale. This price is also sometimes referred to as the List Price or Sticker Price.
  • Marked Price

    = The price tagged on the product. The Marked Price is usually higher than the Selling Price to allow for discounts and profit margins.
  • Example: A shirt is marked with a price tag of $500. This is the Marked Price.

ii. Selling Price (SP)

  • The Selling Price is the actual price at which a product is sold to the customer after any discount or reduction has been applied to the Marked Price. It can either be lower than or equal to the Marked Price, depending on whether there is a discount or if the item is being sold at full price.
  • Selling Price = Marked Price - Discount
  • Example: If the shirt with a Marked Price of $500 is offered with a 20% discount, the Selling Price would be:

    Selling Price (SP) = 500 − (20 / 100 × 500) = 500 − 100 = 400

  • So, the Selling Price after discount is $400.

iii. Relationship Between Marked Price, Selling Price, and Discount

  • The Selling Price (SP) is always related to the Marked Price (MP) through the Discount. The formula is:

    Selling Price = Marked Price − Discount

  • Alternatively, if you know the Discount Percentage (D%), you can calculate the Discount as:

    Discount = (Discount Percentage / 100) × Marked Price

iv. Profit and Loss with Marked Price and Selling Price

  • Profit: If the Selling Price is greater than the Cost Price (CP), a profit is made. Profit is the difference between the Selling Price and the Cost Price:

    Profit = Selling Price (SP) − Cost Price (CP)

  • Loss: If the Selling Price is less than the Cost Price, a loss occurs. Loss is the difference between the Cost Price and the Selling Price:

    Loss = Cost Price (CP) − Selling Price (SP)

  • Profit or Loss Percentage: You can also calculate the profit percentage or loss percentage based on the Cost Price:
    • Profit Percentage = (Profit / Cost Price) × 100
    • Loss Percentage = (Loss / Cost Price) × 100

v. Applications of Marked Price and Selling Price

  • Retail and Discounting: The most common use of Marked Price and Selling Price is in retail, where stores offer discounts on the Marked Price to attract customers, thereby selling at a Selling Price that is lower than the marked or original price.
  • Pricing Strategy: Businesses set the Marked Price to determine how much they want to sell the product for and allow flexibility for offering discounts during promotions or clearance sales.
  • Profit Calculation: Sellers often calculate their Profit by comparing the Selling Price with the Cost Price, which helps in determining the financial success of the transaction.
  • Consumer Shopping: Consumers often compare the Marked Price and the Selling Price to assess the value of discounts or deals offered by retailers.

Definition

  • Interpreting tables and charts is essential in understanding and analyzing data. These tools help visualize complex information, making it easier to spot trends, patterns, and relationships. Here's a guide to interpreting them effectively.

Interpreting Tables

  • A table is a way of presenting data in rows and columns. Each row usually represents an individual record, and each column represents a particular attribute or variable.
  • Steps for Interpreting a Table:
    • Understand the Header: The header row tells you what each column represents (e.g., name, age, sales, etc.).
    • Identify the Data Points: Each cell inside the table contains a data point that corresponds to the row and column's intersection.
    • Look for Patterns or Trends: Scan the rows or columns for increases, decreases, or consistency in values.
    • Calculate Aggregates (if required): You may need to calculate totals, averages, or percentages based on the table.

    • 2020 | 6,000 | 1,000

Interpreting Charts

  • Charts provide a graphical representation of data and can highlight trends and patterns that are harder to identify in a table.

    • Common Types of Charts and Their Interpretation:

    • Bar Chart: A bar chart uses rectangular bars to represent data. The length of each bar corresponds to the value of the variable being represented.
    • Interpretation: Compare the height or length of the bars to understand the relative size of the data.

    • Interpretation: Observe the direction of the line—whether it’s increasing or decreasing, and identify trends over time.
    • Example: A line graph showing the temperature over a week will help you identify the highest and lowest temperatures and whether the temperature is rising or falling.
    • Histogram: A histogram is similar to a bar chart, but it is used to represent the distribution of numerical data.
    • Interpretation: Look at the frequency of data within specific ranges (bins). A taller bar means more data points fall within that range.
    • Example: A histogram of ages will show which age groups have more or fewer individuals.

Tips for Interpreting Tables and Charts

  • Understand the Axes and Labels: For charts, make sure you understand what each axis represents. For tables, know what each row and column signifies.
  • Look for Trends: Are values increasing or decreasing over time? In tables, compare rows to identify this, and in charts, observe the slope of lines or bar heights.
  • Compare Categories: Compare values across rows or columns in tables or bars in charts to identify which category has the highest or lowest value.
  • Context Matters: Always consider the context in which the data is presented. For example, a sudden increase in a bar chart may indicate a seasonal change or a one-time event.
  • Calculate Averages or Totals: In tables, you may need to sum or average data to gain insights (e.g., calculating the total sales over the years).
  • Look for Outliers: Identify any data points that are significantly different from the others. Outliers can be seen as spikes in line charts or bars in bar charts.

4. Data Sufficiency Questions

Definition

  • Data Sufficiency questions test your ability to determine whether you have enough information to answer a given question.
  • The questions typically provide a question followed by two statements (Statement I and Statement II).

Answer Choices

  • The answer choices for Data Sufficiency problems are usually in the following formats:
  • (A) Statement I alone is sufficient, but Statement II alone is not sufficient.
  • (B) Statement II alone is sufficient, but Statement I alone is not sufficient.
  • (C) Both statements together are sufficient, but neither statement alone is sufficient.
  • (D) Each statement alone is sufficient.
  • (E) Both statements together are not sufficient to answer the question.

Steps to Solve Data Sufficiency Questions:

  • Read the Question Carefully:
    • Understand what is being asked.
    • Identify the key variables or relationships in the question.
  • Evaluate Each Statement:
    • Evaluate Statement I on its own to see if it provides enough information to answer the question.
    • Evaluate Statement II in the same way.
    • Consider the possibility of combining both statements to answer the question.
  • Determine Whether Each Statement is Sufficient:
    • After evaluating the statements, determine if you can answer the question based on each statement alone or by combining them.

Example of a Data Sufficiency Question:

Question: What is the value of x?

Statement I: 2x + 3 = 9

Statement II: x - 1 = 3

Step-by-Step Evaluation:

  • Question Understanding: We need to find the value of x.

    • Evaluating Statement I:

    From Statement I: 2x + 3 = 9

    Subtract 3 from both sides: 2x = 6

    Divide both sides by 2: x = 3

    Statement I is sufficient to find x.

    Evaluating Statement II:

    From Statement II: x - 1 = 3

    Add 1 to both sides: x = 4

    Statement II is also sufficient to find x.

    Conclusion:

    Both statements provide sufficient information to answer the question. However, since each statement gives a different value for x, they contradict each other. This suggests that the information provided by the two statements is inconsistent, and we cannot determine the correct value of x.

    Correct Answer: (E) Both statements together are not sufficient.

Types of Data Sufficiency Questions:

i. Arithmetic-based questions:

These questions test your ability to perform basic calculations and interpret mathematical operations.

Example: What is the sum of two numbers?

  • Statement I: The first number is 5.
  • Statement II: The second number is 7.

Solution: Combining both statements gives the sum as 12. So, both statements together are sufficient.

ii. Algebraic equations:

These questions involve algebraic relationships. You may be asked to solve for variables or find relationships between variables.

Example: What is the value of x + y?

  • Statement I: x = 3
  • Statement II: y = 4

Solution: Both statements give the value of the variables separately, so x + y = 7. Hence, both statements together are sufficient.

iii. Geometry-based questions:

These questions test your ability to understand geometric shapes, properties, and relationships.

Example: What is the area of the circle?

  • Statement I: The radius of the circle is 5 cm.
  • Statement II: The circumference of the circle is 31.4 cm.

Solution: Statement I alone gives the radius, and we can use the formula πr² to find the area. Hence, Statement I alone is sufficient.

iv. Word Problems:

These questions often provide real-life scenarios or abstract problems and require you to apply logical reasoning and mathematical knowledge.

Example: How many apples are there in the basket?

  • Statement I: The basket contains 5 apples.
  • Statement II: There are 3 apples in each row, and there are 2 rows.

Solution: Combining both statements gives 3 × 2 = 6. So, both statements together are sufficient.

Tips for Solving Data Sufficiency Questions:

  • Avoid Calculations in the Initial Step: Before jumping into calculations, first evaluate whether the statement itself is sufficient to answer the question.
  • Focus on Logical Consistency: Look for contradictions or inconsistencies in the statements.
  • Consider Combining Statements: Sometimes combining two statements gives a clearer picture. Always check if both statements together provide more than either one individually.
  • Practice: Data sufficiency problems can be tricky, so practice regularly to understand the logic and reasoning behind each question type.

Chapter 5 - Time, Speed & Distance

1. Average Speed & Relative Speed

i. Average Speed

Average Speed is defined as the total distance traveled divided by the total time taken to travel that distance.

Formula for Average Speed:

Average Speed = Total Distance / Total Time

Where:

  • Total Distance = Sum of all distances traveled
  • Total Time = Sum of all times taken

Important Points about Average Speed:

  • Average speed does not depend on the speed at each individual point, but on the total distance and total time.
  • If a person travels at different speeds over different distances or times, the average speed is calculated by considering the total distance and total time taken.

Example 1:

A car travels 100 km at a speed of 50 km/h and then travels another 100 km at a speed of 100 km/h. What is the average speed of the car for the entire journey?

Solution:

  • Distance 1 = 100 km, Speed 1 = 50 km/h, Time 1 = 100 / 50 = 2 hours
  • Distance 2 = 100 km, Speed 2 = 100 km/h, Time 2 = 100 / 100 = 1 hour
  • Total Distance = 100 + 100 = 200 km
  • Total Time = 2 + 1 = 3 hours
  • Average Speed = Total Distance / Total Time = 200 / 3 = 66.67 km/h

Answer: The average speed of the car for the entire journey is 66.67 km/h.

ii. Relative Speed

Relative Speed refers to the speed of one object with respect to another object. It is useful when two objects are moving in the same or opposite directions.

If two objects are moving in the same direction, the relative speed is the difference of their speeds:

Relative Speed = Speed of A - Speed of B (where Speed A is the speed of the faster object and Speed B is the speed of the slower object).

If two objects are moving in opposite directions, the relative speed is the sum of their speeds:

Relative Speed = Speed of A + Speed of B

Example 2:

Two cars are moving towards each other. Car A moves at 60 km/h and Car B moves at 40 km/h. What is their relative speed?

Solution:

  • Since the cars are moving towards each other (opposite directions), the relative speed is:
  • Relative Speed = 60 + 40 = 100 km/h

Answer: The relative speed is 100 km/h.

Example 3:

Two trains are moving in the same direction. Train A moves at 80 km/h and Train B moves at 60 km/h. What is their relative speed?

Solution:

  • Since the trains are moving in the same direction, the relative speed is:
  • Relative Speed = 80 - 60 = 20 km/h

Answer: The relative speed is 20 km/h.

iii. Time Taken to Meet (Relative Speed Approach)

When two objects move towards each other, the time taken for them to meet can be found using the formula:

Time = Distance between them / Relative Speed

Example 4:

Two cars are 200 km apart and are moving towards each other. Car A moves at 60 km/h and Car B moves at 40 km/h. How long will it take for them to meet?

Solution:

  • Relative Speed = 60 + 40 = 100 km/h
  • Distance between them = 200 km
  • Time = 200 / 100 = 2 hours

Answer: It will take 2 hours for the cars to meet.

iv. Time Taken to Cross Each Other (Relative Speed Approach)

When two objects (e.g., trains, cars) are moving in opposite directions and need to pass each other completely, the time taken for them to cross each other is:

Time to Cross = Sum of the lengths of both objects / Relative Speed

Example 5:

Two trains are 100 meters long each and are moving in opposite directions. Train A moves at 60 km/h and Train B moves at 40 km/h. How much time will they take to cross each other?

Solution:

  • Convert the speeds into meters per second:
    • 60 km/h = 60 × 1000 / 3600 = 16.67 m/s
    • 40 km/h = 40 × 1000 / 3600 = 11.11 m/s
  • Relative Speed = 16.67 + 11.11 = 27.78 m/s
  • The total length to be covered is 100 + 100 = 200 meters.
  • Time to Cross = 200 / 27.78 ≈ 7.2 seconds

Answer: The trains will take approximately 7.2 seconds to cross each other.

2. Boats & Streams Problems

Boats & Streams problems are a common type of question in competitive exams involving the concept of relative speed in moving objects. These problems usually involve a boat moving in a river, and the stream’s current influences the boat’s motion. These questions test your ability to calculate the effective speed of the boat and how it is affected by the stream's current.

Key Concepts:

  • Speed of the Boat in Still Water: This is the speed of the boat when there is no current in the river. Denoted as B (speed of boat in still water).
  • Speed of the Stream (Current): This is the speed at which the water of the stream moves. Denoted as S (speed of stream).
  • Effective Speed of the Boat in the Stream:
    • When the boat is moving downstream (in the direction of the current), the current assists the boat, so the effective speed is the sum of the speed of the boat in still water and the speed of the stream: Effective Speed Downstream = B + S
    • When the boat is moving upstream (against the current), the current opposes the boat, so the effective speed is the difference between the speed of the boat in still water and the speed of the stream: Effective Speed Upstream = B - S

Types of Boats & Streams Problems:

  • Speed of the Boat & Stream: These problems usually ask you to calculate the speed of the boat or the stream when the effective speed downstream or upstream is given.
  • Time Taken to Travel a Certain Distance: These problems involve calculating the time taken by the boat to cover a certain distance, either downstream or upstream.
  • Meeting or Crossing Point: Sometimes, two boats moving in opposite or the same direction are asked to meet or cross each other. The speed of the stream affects their meeting time.

Examples and Solutions:

Example 1:

A boat travels 40 km downstream in 4 hours and 40 km upstream in 5 hours. What is the speed of the boat in still water and the speed of the stream?

Solution:

Downstream Speed:

  • Distance = 40 km, Time = 4 hours
  • Speed downstream = 40 / 4 = 10 km/h

Upstream Speed:

  • Distance = 40 km, Time = 5 hours
  • Speed upstream = 40 / 5 = 8 km/h

Let the speed of the boat in still water be B km/h, and the speed of the stream be S km/h.

  • Downstream Speed: B + S = 10
  • Upstream Speed: B - S = 8

By solving these two equations:

  • (B + S) = 10
  • (B - S) = 8

Add both equations:

  • 2B = 18 ⇒ B = 9 km/h

Substitute B = 9 into B + S = 10:

  • 9 + S = 10 ⇒ S = 1 km/h

Answer: The speed of the boat in still water is 9 km/h, and the speed of the stream is 1 km/h.

3. Trains & Platform Problems

Trains & Platform problems are another common category of questions in competitive exams. These problems generally involve the motion of trains passing a platform or crossing each other. To solve such problems, you need to apply concepts related to relative speed, time, and distance.

Key Concepts:

  • Speed of the Train: This is the speed at which the train is moving. Denoted as S (Speed of the train).
  • Length of the Train: This is the total length of the train. Denoted as Ltrain (Length of the train).
  • Length of the Platform: This is the total length of the platform. Denoted as Lplatform (Length of the platform).
  • Time Taken to Pass the Platform: This is the time taken by the train to completely pass the platform. Denoted as Tpass (Time taken to pass the platform).

Basic Formulas:

  • Time Taken to Pass a Platform: When a train passes a platform, the total distance the train covers is the sum of the length of the train and the length of the platform. The time taken to pass the platform is given by:
    • Time = (Ltrain + Lplatform) / S
  • Time Taken to Cross Each Other: When two trains pass each other, the total distance they cover is the sum of their lengths, and the relative speed (if they are moving in opposite directions) is the sum of their individual speeds. If they are moving in the same direction, the relative speed is the difference of their individual speeds:
    • Time to Cross = (Sum of the lengths of both trains) / Relative Speed

Types of Trains & Platform Problems:

  • Time Taken to Pass a Platform: These problems involve calculating how much time a train will take to pass a platform given the speed and length of the train and platform.
  • Time Taken to Cross Another Train: In these problems, two trains are moving towards each other or in the same direction. You need to calculate the time taken for them to cross each other.

Examples and Solutions:

Example 1:

A train, 100 meters long, is moving at a speed of 36 km/h. How much time will it take to pass a platform that is 200 meters long?

Solution:

Step 1: Convert the speed of the train from km/h to m/s:

36 km/h = (36 × 1000) / 3600 = 10 m/s

Step 2: Calculate the total distance to be covered:

Total Distance = 100 m (train length) + 200 m (platform length) = 300 m

Step 3: Calculate the time taken to pass the platform:

Time = Total Distance / Speed = 300 m / 10 m/s = 30 seconds

Answer: The train will take 30 seconds to pass the platform.

4. Circular Motion & Races Problems

Circular motion problems and race-related problems are commonly found in competitive exams. These problems typically involve understanding concepts related to the motion of objects along circular paths (like a car on a circular track) or the time it takes for different objects to cover specific distances, such as in races.

Circular Motion Problems

Circular motion refers to the motion of an object in a circular path. When objects move in a circle, they are said to undergo uniform circular motion if their speed remains constant. For objects moving in circular paths, key concepts like angular velocity, linear velocity, and centripetal force come into play.

Key Concepts of Circular Motion:

  • Linear Velocity (Tangential Speed): The speed of an object moving along a circular path, denoted by v. It is the distance traveled per unit time along the circumference of the circle.
    • v = r * ω
  • Angular Velocity (ω): The rate at which an object moves through an angle in a circular path. It is measured in radians per second (rad/s).
    • ω = θ / t
  • Centripetal Force: The force that keeps an object moving in a circular path is called the centripetal force. This force is always directed toward the center of the circle.
    • F = m * v² / r
  • Period of Revolution (T): The time taken for one complete revolution of the object around the circular path.
    • T = 2πr / v
  • Frequency (f): The number of revolutions completed per unit time. It is the reciprocal of the period.
    • f = 1 / T

Types of Circular Motion Problems:

  • Time Taken for One Complete Revolution: These problems involve calculating the time taken for an object to complete one full circle along a circular track or path.
  • Speed of an Object in Circular Motion: These problems involve finding the linear speed (tangential speed) or angular velocity of an object moving in a circular path.

Examples and Solutions:

Example 1:

A car is moving along a circular track with a radius of 100 meters. If the car completes one full revolution in 20 seconds, what is the speed of the car?

Solution:

Step 1: The distance covered in one revolution is the circumference of the circle:

Circumference = 2πr = 2π × 100 = 628.31 meters

Step 2: The time taken for one revolution is 20 seconds.

Step 3: The speed v is the distance divided by the time:

v = Circumference / Time = 628.31 / 20 = 31.42 m/s

Answer: The speed of the car is 31.42 m/s.

Chapter 6 - Time & Work

1. Work, Efficiency, and Wages Problems

Work, efficiency, and wages problems are commonly found in competitive exams, especially in quantitative reasoning sections. These problems involve concepts of time, work, and the amount of money earned based on the work done.

Key Concepts:

Work:

Work refers to the completion of a task or job. In these problems, the work is generally expressed in terms of the "amount of work" or "units of work."

Efficiency:

Efficiency refers to how much work a person or machine can complete in a given time. In work-related problems, efficiency is often stated as a fraction of the total work done per unit of time.

Time:

Time is the duration taken to complete the work. The time taken to complete the work depends on the efficiency of the worker or machine.

Wages:

Wages refer to the payment for work done. In these problems, wages are generally calculated based on the amount of work completed, and the amount paid is proportional to the work done.

Formulae:

Work-Rate Relationship:

If a person completes a work in T days, the work rate of that person is 1/T of the total work per day.

Work Rate of A = 1 / TA work/day
Work Rate of B = 1 / TB work/day

Combined Work Rate:

When two or more people work together, their combined rate of work is the sum of their individual rates.

Combined Rate = 1 / TA + 1 / TB

Work Formula:

Work is the product of rate and time. So, if a person works for t days at a rate of r, the total work done is:

Work = r × t

Wages Calculation:

Wages are often directly proportional to the work done. If a person completes a fraction 1/n of the work, they receive 1/n of the total wages. If the total wages for the full work are W, the wage earned for completing 1/n of the work is: 

Wages = (Work Done / Total Work) × W

Types of Problems on Work, Efficiency, and Wages:

  • Individual Work and Time Problems: Calculate how long it takes for an individual to complete a task based on their efficiency.
  • Combined Work Problems: When multiple workers work together, find the total time taken or total work done.
  • Wage Calculation Problems: Calculate the wages earned based on the work done by an individual or a group of workers.
  • Work and Time Ratio Problems: These problems require comparing the times taken by different people based on their work rates.

Examples and Solutions:

Example 1: Individual Work Time Problem

If person A can complete a piece of work in 12 days, and B can complete the same work in 18 days, how long will it take for them to complete the work together?

Solution:

The rate of work of A is:

Rate of A = 1 / 12 work/day

The rate of work of B is:

Rate of B = 1 / 18 work/day

The combined rate of A and B working together is:

Combined Rate = 1 / 12 + 1 / 18 = 5 / 36 work/day

The time taken for them to complete the work together is:

Time taken = 1 / (5 / 36) = 36 / 5 = 7.2 days

So, A and B will complete the work in 7.2 days.

2. Pipes & Cisterns Problems

Pipes and cisterns problems deal with the filling or emptying of a tank or cistern using pipes with different rates of flow. The key concepts are related to the rates at which water flows into or out of the tank, and the time taken to fill or empty the tank. These problems are often based on the concepts of work, efficiency, and rates.

Key Concepts:

Filling and Emptying:

A pipe filling the cistern is considered to add water to the cistern. A pipe emptying the cistern is considered to remove water from the cistern.

Rate of Flow:

If a pipe fills the cistern in T hours, its rate of flow is 1/T cisterns per hour. If a pipe empties the cistern in T hours, its rate of flow is -1/T cisterns per hour (negative because it's emptying).

Combined Rates:

When multiple pipes are working together, the total rate of flow is the sum of the individual rates. If a pipe fills the cistern and another empties it, the combined rate is the difference between the filling and emptying rates.

Combined Rate = 1 / T1 - 1 / T2

Where T1 is the time taken to fill the cistern and T2 is the time taken to empty it.

Time Taken:

The time taken to fill or empty the cistern is the inverse of the combined rate of the pipes.

Formulas:

Filling Rate:

Filling Rate = 1 / T

Where T is the time taken to fill the cistern.

Emptying Rate:

Emptying Rate = -1 / T

Where T is the time taken to empty the cistern.

Combined Rate (When pipes are working together):

Combined Rate = Filling Rate + Emptying Rate

If the pipes are working against each other, the emptying rate is negative.

Time to Fill or Empty:

Time to Fill or Empty = 1 / Combined Rate

Types of Problems:

  • Filling or Emptying the Cistern: These problems ask how long it takes for a pipe or combination of pipes to fill or empty the cistern.
  • Combined Filling and Emptying: These problems involve two or more pipes working together (one filling, one emptying), and the goal is to find the time taken to fill or empty the cistern.
  • Time Required When a Pipe is Turned Off: These problems ask for the time it will take to fill the cistern after one pipe has been turned off, or when only one pipe remains open.

Examples and Solutions:

Example 1: Filling and Emptying Problem

A pipe can fill a cistern in 10 hours, while another pipe can empty the same cistern in 15 hours. How long will it take to fill the cistern if both pipes are open?

Solution:

The filling rate of the first pipe (filling pipe) is:

1 / 10 cistern/hour

The emptying rate of the second pipe (emptying pipe) is:

-1 / 15 cistern/hour

The combined rate of both pipes working together is:

1 / 10 - 1 / 15 = 3 / 30 - 2 / 30 = 1 / 30 cistern/hour

The time taken to fill the cistern is:

Time = 1 / (1 / 30) = 30 hours

So, it will take 30 hours to fill the cistern when both pipes are open.

3. Work & Time Together

Work and time problems are common in quantitative aptitude, and they deal with the amount of work completed over time by individuals or groups of workers, machines, or other entities. The key concepts involve finding the total time taken, the amount of work done, or the rate at which work is completed when multiple workers or entities are involved.

Key Concepts:

Rate of Work:

The rate at which work is completed is typically expressed as "work per unit of time." If a worker completes a job in T hours, their rate of work is 1/T of the total work per hour.

Total Work:

The total amount of work is usually taken as 1 unit (representing the complete task).

Combined Work:

When two or more people work together, the total rate of work is the sum of their individual rates of work. If they are working on the same task, the combined work rate is the sum of their individual work rates.

Time Taken:

The time taken to complete the work is the inverse of the combined work rate.

Formulae:

Work Formula:

Work = Rate of Work × Time

Individual Work Rates:

If a person or machine takes T hours to complete the work, their rate of work is 1/T work per hour.

Combined Work Rate:

When two or more workers work together, the combined rate is the sum of their individual rates:

Combined Rate = 1 / T1 + 1 / T2 + …

Where T1, T2, etc., are the times taken by individuals working separately.

Time Taken to Complete the Work Together:

Time Taken = 1 / Combined Rate

Types of Problems:

  • Individual Work and Time: When one worker completes the task alone, calculate the time or the rate at which they work.
  • Combined Work of Multiple Workers: When multiple workers are involved, calculate how long it takes to complete the work when they work together.
  • Work Done in a Given Time: Determine the work done by multiple workers in a specific amount of time.
  • Time Taken After Some Work is Done: Calculate the remaining time after some work has already been done, either by one or more workers.

Examples and Solutions:

Example 1: Individual Work Time

A can complete a work in 8 days, and B can complete the same work in 12 days. How long will it take for A and B to complete the work if they work together?

Solution:

The rate of work of A is:

1 / 8 work/day

The rate of work of B is:

1 / 12 work/day

The combined rate of A and B is:

1 / 8 + 1 / 12 = 3 / 24 + 2 / 24 = 5 / 24 work/day

The time taken to complete the work is:

Time = 1 / (5 / 24) = 24 / 5 = 4.8 days

So, A and B together will complete the work in 4.8 days.

4. Work & Wages Distribution

Work and wages distribution problems are an extension of work and time problems. These problems deal with the distribution of wages among workers based on the amount of work they complete. The basic principle is that workers are paid according to the amount of work they perform, and if multiple workers work together, their wages are distributed based on the amount of work each one does.

Key Concepts:

Rate of Work:

The rate of work represents the amount of work completed per unit of time. If a worker completes a task in T days, their rate of work is 1/T of the work per day.

Total Work:

Total work is usually considered as 1 unit of the task or job.

Wages Distribution:

If the total wages for the task is W, the wages are distributed among the workers based on the proportion of work done by each worker.

If A, B, and C work together to complete a job, their wages are distributed based on the amount of work each worker contributes.

Work Done and Wages:

The share of wages for a worker is proportional to the work done by them. If Worker A's rate of work is 1/TA, Worker B's rate of work is 1/TB, and Worker C's rate of work is 1/TC, then the total work done by all workers is 1 unit.

Formulae:

Work Done by Each Worker:

If the total work is 1 unit, the work done by each worker is based on their rate of work relative to the total rate of work.

Work done by Worker A in 1 day = 1 / TA
Work done by Worker B in 1 day = 1 / TB
Work done by Worker C in 1 day = 1 / TC

Total Rate of Work:

Total Rate = 1 / TA + 1 / TB + 1 / TC

Wages Distribution:

The share of wages for each worker is proportional to the amount of work done by them. If the total wages are W, the wages for each worker will be:

Wages for A = W × (1 / TA) / Total Rate
Wages for B = W × (1 / TB) / Total Rate
Wages for C = W × (1 / TC) / Total Rate

Types of Problems:

  • Wages Distribution Among Workers: Given the total wages and the time taken by each worker, calculate how much each worker should be paid based on the work done.
  • Wages Distribution Based on Time: In some problems, workers may work for different amounts of time, and their wages are distributed based on the time worked.
  • Partial Work Completion: Sometimes, workers may leave before the work is completed, and the wages are distributed based on the amount of work done by each worker until they leave.

Examples and Solutions:

Example 1: Wages Distribution Based on Work Done

A and B together can complete a task in 6 days. If A can complete the task alone in 8 days, how should the wages of 240 be distributed between A and B?

Solution:

The rate of work of A is 1/8 (A completes the task in 8 days).

The rate of work of A and B together is 1/6 (A and B together complete the task in 6 days).

The rate of work of B alone is:

Rate of B = 1/6 - 1/8 = 4/24 - 3/24 = 1/24

So, B takes 24 days to complete the task alone.

The work done by A in one day is 1/8 and the work done by B in one day is 1/24.

The total work rate is:

Total Rate = 1/8 + 1/24 = 3/24 + 1/24 = 4/24 = 1/6

The share of wages for A is proportional to A's work rate:

Wages for A = 240 × (1/8) / (1/6) = 240 × (6/8) = 240 × (3/4) = 180

The share of wages for B is proportional to B's work rate:

Wages for B = 240 × (1/24) / (1/6) = 240 × (6/24) = 240 × (1/4) = 60

So, A should receive 180 and B should receive 60 from the total wages of 240.

5. Negative Work & Reverse Work

In physics and mathematics, negative work and reverse work refer to situations where the work done by a force is in the opposite direction of the movement of an object or when a worker is effectively reducing the amount of work done. These concepts are essential when analyzing forces acting on objects and understanding how different forces influence the movement or energy of a system.

i. Negative Work

Negative work occurs when the force applied to an object is in the opposite direction of the object's displacement. When this happens, the object moves in the opposite direction to the force applied, and the work done is considered negative.

Concept of Negative Work:

Work is calculated as:

W = F × d × cos(θ)

where:

  • W is the work done
  • F is the magnitude of the force applied
  • d is the displacement of the object
  • θ is the angle between the force and the displacement vector

Negative work occurs when θ > 90°. In such cases, the force acts opposite to the direction of displacement, and the cosine of an angle greater than 90 degrees is negative.

Example of Negative Work:

If a person is pushing a box to the right, but friction is acting in the opposite direction (to the left), then friction does negative work. The force of friction slows down the box as it moves to the right.

In this case:

  • The displacement of the box is to the right (positive direction).
  • The force of friction acts to the left (negative direction).
  • The angle between the displacement and force direction is 180°, which makes cos(180°) = -1, so the work done by friction is negative.

Mathematical Example:

If a box of mass 5 kg is pushed to the right for 10 meters, and the frictional force is 2 N acting opposite to the direction of motion:

W = F × d × cos(180°)
W = 2 × 10 × (-1) = -20 Joules

The work done by the friction is -20 Joules, indicating that energy is being taken away from the box.

ii. Reverse Work

Reverse work refers to a scenario where the direction of the force applied is reversed after performing positive work. It can occur in situations where a worker or machine begins to reverse the process that was initially done (i.e., reducing the effect of work done earlier).

Reverse Work in Human Effort:

In the context of human labor, reverse work can be interpreted as situations where a person who was initially performing a task (e.g., lifting or carrying an object) stops the task or does the opposite (e.g., lowering or pushing the object back).

Example of Reverse Work:

Consider a scenario where you lift a box (positive work) and then lower it (reverse work):

  • Lifting: Work is done in the direction of the applied force (positive work).
  • Lowering: Work is done in the opposite direction, and the force is exerted downward to counteract gravity. This results in negative or reverse work.

Work and Energy Concept:

In reverse work, energy is transferred in the opposite direction. For instance, lowering a lifted object means the gravitational potential energy is reduced, and the energy is given back to the environment.

Mathematical Example:

If you lift a box with a force of 50 N for 2 meters, the work done is:

W = 50 × 2 = 100 Joules (positive work)

If you then lower the box by 2 meters with the same force, the reverse work is:

W = 50 × 2 = 100 Joules (negative or reverse work)

The reverse work here takes away the energy that was initially imparted to the box by lifting it.

Practical Examples of Negative and Reverse Work:

Example 1: Negative Work in a Car Braking:

When a car is moving forward, and the brakes are applied, the friction between the tires and the road slows down the car. This is an example of negative work. The car moves in the direction of its velocity, while the frictional force opposes the motion. The brakes are performing negative work by reducing the car’s kinetic energy.

Basic Probability Concepts

Probability is a branch of mathematics that deals with calculating the likelihood or chance of an event occurring. It is widely used in various fields such as statistics, finance, gaming, and science.

i. What is Probability?

Probability is the measure of how likely an event is to occur, expressed as a number between 0 and 1.

The probability of an event (P(E)) is given by:

P(E) = Number of favorable outcomes / Total number of possible outcomes

where:

  • P(E) is the probability of event E.
  • The numerator is the number of favorable outcomes (the outcomes we are interested in).
  • The denominator is the total number of possible outcomes (all outcomes that could possibly occur).

ii. Types of Events

Certain Event:

An event that is guaranteed to occur.

Example: The probability of rolling a number between 1 and 6 on a standard die is 1 (i.e., P(Rolling 1-6) = 1).

Impossible Event:

An event that cannot occur.

Example: The probability of rolling a 7 on a standard 6-sided die is 0 (i.e., P(Rolling 7) = 0).

Simple Event:

An event that has only one outcome.

Example: Rolling a 4 on a standard die.

Compound Event:

An event that involves two or more simple events happening together.

Example: Rolling an even number (i.e., 2, 4, or 6) on a standard die.

iii. Basic Probability Rules

The Probability of the Sample Space:

The probability of the entire sample space (all possible outcomes) is always 1.

P(Sample Space) = 1

The Probability of Complementary Events:

The probability of the complement of an event A (i.e., the event that A does not occur) is given by: 

P(A') = 1 - P(A)

where P(A') is the probability of event A not happening.

Addition Rule:

The addition rule is used to find the probability of the occurrence of either of two events. For two events A and B:

  • For mutually exclusive events (events that cannot happen at the same time):
    P(A ∪ B) = P(A) + P(B)
  • For non-mutually exclusive events (events that can happen at the same time):
    P(A ∪ B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A ∩ B)

Multiplication Rule:

The multiplication rule is used to find the probability of two independent events happening together:

  • For independent events (events that do not affect each other):
    P(A ∩ B) = P(A) × P(B)
  • For dependent events (events where the occurrence of one affects the occurrence of the other):
    P(A ∩ B) = P(A) × P(B|A)

where P(B|A) is the conditional probability of B occurring given that A has already occurred.

iv. Conditional Probability

Conditional probability is the probability of an event occurring given that another event has already occurred. It is represented as P(A|B), the probability of event A occurring given event B.

The formula for conditional probability is:

P(A|B) = P(A ∩ B) / P(B)

where:

  • P(A ∩ B) is the probability that both A and B occur.
  • P(B) is the probability that event B occurs.

v. Sample Space and Events

The sample space (denoted S) is the set of all possible outcomes of a random experiment. An event is any subset of the sample space.

Example 1: Rolling a Die

Sample space S = {1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6}

Event A: Rolling an even number A = {2, 4, 6}

Event B: Rolling a number greater than 4 B = {5, 6}

The probability of event A is:

P(A) = Number of favorable outcomes for A / Total number of outcomes = 3 / 6 = 0.5

Example 2: Tossing a Coin

Sample space S = {H, T} (H for heads, T for tails)

Event A: Getting heads A = {H}

The probability of event A is:

P(A) = 1 / 2 = 0.5

vi. Types of Probability

Theoretical Probability:

This is based on the assumption that all outcomes are equally likely. For example, when tossing a fair coin, the probability of getting heads is 1/2 because there are two equally likely outcomes: heads or tails.

Experimental Probability:

This is based on observed outcomes of an experiment. It is calculated as the ratio of the number of times an event occurs to the total number of trials.

P(E) = Number of favorable outcomes / Total number of trials

For example, if a die is rolled 100 times and the number 6 appears 20 times, the experimental probability of rolling a 6 is 20/100 = 0.2.

Empirical Probability:

Similar to experimental probability, but typically based on a larger set of data or experiments.

vii. Law of Large Numbers

The Law of Large Numbers states that as the number of trials or experiments increases, the experimental probability of an event will approach the theoretical probability. For example, if you flip a fair coin many times, the proportion of heads should approach 0.5 as the number of flips increases.

viii. Probability Distribution

A probability distribution is a function that gives the probability of each outcome in a sample space. There are two main types of probability distributions:

  • Discrete Probability Distribution: For events with a finite number of possible outcomes (e.g., rolling a die).
  • Continuous Probability Distribution: For events with an infinite number of possible outcomes (e.g., the probability of selecting a number from a continuous range).

2. Mean, Median, and Mode

Definition

  • Mean, Median, and Mode are measures of central tendency that summarize a dataset by identifying a "typical" value.

  • Formula: Mean = ΣX n

    where ΣX = sum of all values, n = number of values.

SI Unit

Median

  • Example (Odd number of values): Dataset = 7, 2, 9, 3, 5. Ordered = 2, 3, 5, 7, 9. Median = 5.
  • Example (Even number of values): Dataset = 4, 2, 8, 10, 6, 12. Ordered = 2, 4, 6, 8, 10, 12. Median = (6 + 8) / 2 = 7.

3. Variance and Standard Deviation

Definition

  • Variance and Standard Deviation are measures of spread or dispersion in a dataset.
  • They describe how much the data points in a dataset differ from the mean (average) of the dataset.

Variance

  • The **variance** measures the average squared deviation of each data point from the mean.
  • Formula for **Population Variance**: Variance ( σ ² ) = Σ(Xi - μ)2 N

    Where σ² = population variance, Xi = each data point, μ = population mean, N = total number of data points.

  • Formula for **Sample Variance**: Variance ( s ² ) = Σ(Xi - X̄)2 n - 1

    Where s² = sample variance, Xi = each data point, X̄ = sample mean, n = total number of data points, and (n-1) is for Bessel's correction.

  • Steps to Calculate Variance:
    • Find the mean of the dataset.
    • Subtract the mean from each data point and square the result.
    • Find the average of these squared deviations.
  • Example (Population Variance):
    • Dataset = 2, 4, 6, 8, 10
    • Mean (μ) = (2 + 4 + 6 + 8 + 10) / 5 = 6
    • Squared deviations from the mean: (2-6)² = 16, (4-6)² = 4, (6-6)² = 0, (8-6)² = 4, (10-6)² = 16
    • Variance = (16 + 4 + 0 + 4 + 16) / 5 = 40 / 5 = 8

Standard Deviation

  • The **standard deviation** is the square root of the variance. It provides a measure of the spread of data points in the same unit as the data.
  • Formula for **Population Standard Deviation**: Standard   Deviation ( σ ) = Σ(Xi - μ)2 N
  • Formula for **Sample Standard Deviation**: Standard   Deviation ( s ) = Σ(Xi - X̄)2 n - 1
  • Steps to Calculate Standard Deviation:
    • Find the variance of the dataset.
    • Take the square root of the variance.
  • Example (Population Standard Deviation):
    • Given the dataset: 2, 4, 6, 8, 10, and variance = 8, the standard deviation is:
    • Standard Deviation = √8 ≈ 2.83

4. Permutations and Combinations

Definition

  • Permutations and Combinations are fundamental concepts in combinatorics, the branch of mathematics dealing with counting and arrangements.
  • These concepts are used to determine the number of ways to arrange or select items from a set.

Permutations

  • A **permutation** is an arrangement of objects in a specific order. The order matters in permutations.
  • Formula for **Permutations**: P ( n , r ) = n ! (n - r) !

    Where:
    n = Total number of objects,
    r = Number of objects to arrange,
    ! (Factorial) is the product of all positive integers up to that number (e.g., 5! = 5 × 4 × 3 × 2 × 1 = 120).

  • Example:
    • If you have 5 books and want to know how many ways you can arrange 3 of them on a shelf:
    • P ( 5 , 3 ) = 5 ! (5 - 3) !

      Which simplifies to: 5 × 4 × 3 = 60 ways to arrange 3 books from 5.

  • Key Points About Permutations:
    • The order of arrangement matters.
    • Used for arranging items in a specific order.
    • If the objects are identical, divide by the factorial of the number of identical objects.

Combinations

  • A **combination** is a selection of objects where the order does not matter.
  • Formula for **Combinations**: C ( n , r ) = n ! r ! (n - r) !

    Where:
    n = Total number of objects,
    r = Number of objects to choose,
    ! (Factorial) is the product of all positive integers up to that number.

  • Example:
    • If you have 5 books and want to know how many ways you can choose 3 books from them (without considering order):
    • C ( 5 , 3 ) = 5 ! 3 ! 2 !

      Which simplifies to: 5 × 4 / 2 × 1 = 10 ways to choose 3 books from 5.

  • Key Points About Combinations:
    • The order of selection does not matter.
    • Used for selecting items without regard to order.
    • The formula accounts for the fact that the same selection can appear in different orders.

3. Coordinate Geometry Basics

Definition

  • Coordinate Geometry (also called Analytical Geometry) uses a coordinate system to represent geometric shapes and solve problems related to lines, curves, and shapes using algebraic equations.
  • It combines algebra and geometry by using coordinates on a plane to represent points and define shapes.

The Cartesian Plane

  • The Cartesian plane is a 2D grid formed by two perpendicular number lines, the X-axis (horizontal) and the Y-axis (vertical).
  • The point of intersection of the X-axis and Y-axis is called the origin, denoted as \( O(0, 0) \).
  • The plane is divided into four quadrants:
    1. Quadrant I: Both \( x \) and \( y \) are positive.
    2. Quadrant II: \( x \) is negative, \( y \) is positive.
    3. Quadrant III: Both \( x \) and \( y \) are negative.
    4. Quadrant IV: \( x \) is positive, \( y \) is negative.

Coordinates of a Point

  • The position of any point in the plane is represented by an ordered pair \( (x, y) \), where:
    • \( x \) is the horizontal distance from the origin (along the X-axis).
    • \( y \) is the vertical distance from the origin (along the Y-axis).
  • Example: Point \( A(3, 4) \) is 3 units to the right and 4 units above the origin.

Distance Formula

  • The distance between two points \( P(x_1, y_1) \) and \( Q(x_2, y_2) \) is given by the formula:

    Formula: \( d = \sqrt{(x_2 - x_1)^2 + (y_2 - y_1)^2} \)

  • Example: The distance between \( A(3, 4) \) and \( B(-2, -5) \) is approximately 10.29 units.

Midpoint Formula

  • The midpoint \( M \) of a line segment joining \( P(x_1, y_1) \) and \( Q(x_2, y_2) \) is given by:

    Formula: \( M = \left( \frac{x_1 + x_2}{2}, \frac{y_1 + y_2}{2} \right) \)

  • Example: The midpoint of \( A(3, 4) \) and \( B(-2, -5) \) is \( M(0.5, -0.5) \).

Slope of a Line

  • The slope \( m \) of the line through points \( P(x_1, y_1) \) and \( Q(x_2, y_2) \) is given by:

    Formula: \( m = \frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1} \)

  • The slope determines the steepness of the line:
    • If \( m > 0 \), the line slopes upwards from left to right.
    • If \( m < 0 \), the line slopes downwards from left to right.
    • If \( m = 0 \), the line is horizontal.
    • If the slope is undefined, the line is vertical.

Equation of a Line

  • The equation of a line can be expressed in different forms:
    • Slope-Intercept Form: \( y = mx + c \), where \( m \) is the slope and \( c \) is the y-intercept.
    • Point-Slope Form: \( y - y_1 = m(x - x_1) \), where \( (x_1, y_1) \) is a point on the line and \( m \) is the slope.

Collinearity of Points

  • Three points \( A(x_1, y_1) \), \( B(x_2, y_2) \), and \( C(x_3, y_3) \) are collinear if the area of the triangle formed by these points is zero.

    Area Formula: \( \text{Area} = \frac{1}{2} \left| x_1(y_2 - y_3) + x_2(y_3 - y_1) + x_3(y_1 - y_2) \right| \)

Area of a Triangle

  • The area of a triangle with vertices \( A(x_1, y_1) \), \( B(x_2, y_2) \), and \( C(x_3, y_3) \) is:

    Area Formula: \( \text{Area} = \frac{1}{2} \left| x_1(y_2 - y_3) + x_2(y_3 - y_1) + x_3(y_1 - y_2) \right| \)

General Form of the Equation of a Line

  • The general form of the equation of a line is:

    Formula: \( Ax + By + C = 0 \), where \( A \), \( B \), and \( C \) are constants.

  • To convert from slope-intercept form \( y = mx + c \) to general form, rearrange the equation to \( mx - y + c = 0 \).

4. Properties of Circles & Polygons

Properties of Circles

  • A circle is a collection of all points in a plane that are equidistant from a fixed point, called the center. The distance from the center to any point on the circle is called the radius.

Key Properties:

Radius:

  • The distance from the center of the circle to any point on the circle.
  • All radii of a circle are equal.

Diameter:

  • The longest chord of the circle, passing through the center.
  • It is twice the radius:

    Formula: \( D = 2r \), where \( r \) is the radius.

Circumference:

  • The perimeter or the boundary length of the circle.
  • Formula: \( C = 2\pi r \), where \( r \) is the radius of the circle.

Area:

  • The space enclosed by the circle.
  • Formula: \( A = \pi r^2 \), where \( r \) is the radius.

Chord:

  • A line segment joining two points on the circle.
  • The diameter is the longest chord of the circle.

Secant:

  • A straight line that intersects the circle at two points.

Tangent:

  • A straight line that touches the circle at exactly one point (called the point of tangency).
  • The tangent is perpendicular to the radius at the point of tangency.

Arc:

  • A part of the circumference of the circle.
  • It can be a minor arc (less than 180°) or a major arc (greater than 180°).

Sector:

  • A region bounded by two radii and the arc between them.
  • Formula for Area of Sector: \( A = \frac{\theta}{360^\circ} \times \pi r^2 \), where \( \theta \) is the central angle of the sector.

Segment:

  • A region bounded by a chord and the arc between the two endpoints of the chord.

Central Angle:

  • An angle whose vertex is at the center of the circle, and whose sides pass through two points on the circle.

Inscribed Angle:

  • An angle whose vertex is on the circle and whose sides are chords of the circle.

Properties of Polygons

  • A polygon is a closed, two-dimensional shape with straight sides. Polygons can have any number of sides, and they are classified based on the number of sides they have.

1. Triangle (3 sides)

  • The sum of the interior angles is always \( 180^\circ \).
  • Types of triangles:
    • Equilateral Triangle: All sides and angles are equal (each angle is \( 60^\circ \)).
    • Isosceles Triangle: Two sides are equal, and the angles opposite those sides are equal.
    • Scalene Triangle: All sides and angles are different.

2. Quadrilateral (4 sides)

  • The sum of the interior angles is always \( 360^\circ \).
  • Types of quadrilaterals:
    • Square: All sides and angles are equal (each angle is \( 90^\circ \)).
    • Rectangle: Opposite sides are equal, and all angles are \( 90^\circ \).
    • Parallelogram: Opposite sides are equal and parallel, and opposite angles are equal.
    • Rhombus: All sides are equal, but angles are not necessarily \( 90^\circ \).
    • Trapezium: Only one pair of opposite sides is parallel.

3. Pentagon (5 sides)

  • The sum of the interior angles is \( 540^\circ \).
  • Regular pentagon: All sides and angles are equal.

4. Hexagon (6 sides)

  • The sum of the interior angles is \( 720^\circ \).
  • Regular hexagon: All sides and angles are equal. The interior angle is \( 120^\circ \).

5. Heptagon (7 sides)

  • The sum of the interior angles is \( 900^\circ \).

6. Octagon (8 sides)

  • The sum of the interior angles is \( 1080^\circ \).
  • Regular octagon: All sides and angles are equal. The interior angle is \( 135^\circ \).

7. Polygon with n sides

  • The sum of the interior angles of an n-sided polygon is \( (n - 2) \times 180^\circ \).
  • The exterior angles of any polygon always add up to \( 360^\circ \).

Special Properties of Regular Polygons

  • A regular polygon is a polygon where all sides and angles are equal. The most notable properties include:

Interior Angle:

  • The formula for the interior angle of a regular polygon with \( n \) sides is:

    Formula: \( \text{Interior Angle} = \frac{(n - 2) \times 180^\circ}{n} \)

Exterior Angle:

  • The formula for the exterior angle of a regular polygon with \( n \) sides is:

    Formula: \( \text{Exterior Angle} = \frac{360^\circ}{n} \)

Area of Regular Polygon:

  • The area of a regular polygon can be calculated using the formula:

    Formula: \( A = \frac{1}{4}n \times s^2 \times \cot\left(\frac{\pi}{n}\right) \), where \( n \) is the number of sides and \( s \) is the length of each side.

5. Trigonometry Basics

Definition

  • Trigonometry is the branch of mathematics that deals with the relationships between the angles and sides of triangles, particularly right-angled triangles.
  • It involves six primary trigonometric ratios: sine, cosine, tangent, cosecant, secant, and cotangent.

Trigonometric Ratios

  • For a right-angled triangle with an angle 𝜃:
  • Hypotenuse = H (the side opposite the right angle)
  • Opposite side = O (the side opposite the angle 𝜃)
  • Adjacent side = A (the side adjacent to the angle 𝜃)
  • Sine (sin):
    sin𝜃 = O/H
  • Cosine (cos):
    cos𝜃 = A/H
  • Tangent (tan):
    tan𝜃 = O/A
  • Cosecant (csc):
    csc𝜃 = H/O
  • Secant (sec):
    sec𝜃 = H/A
  • Cotangent (cot):
    cot𝜃 = A/O

SI Units

  • There is no specific SI unit for trigonometric ratios as they are dimensionless.

Important Trigonometric Identities

  • Pythagorean Identity:
    sin²𝜃 + cos²𝜃 = 1
  • Tangent and Secant Identity:
    tan²𝜃 + 1 = sec²𝜃
  • Cotangent and Cosecant Identity:
    1 + cot²𝜃 = csc²𝜃

Special Angles in Trigonometry

  • 0°:
    sin0° = 0, cos0° = 1, tan0° = 0
  • 30°:
    sin30° = 1/2, cos30° = √3/2, tan30° = 1/√3
  • 45°:
    sin45° = √2/2, cos45° = √2/2, tan45° = 1
  • 60°:
    sin60° = √3/2, cos60° = 1/2, tan60° = √3
  • 90°:
    sin90° = 1, cos90° = 0, tan90° = ∞ (undefined)

Trigonometric Equations

  • Trigonometric equations involve finding angles that satisfy a given trigonometric expression.
  • Basic strategies for solving include:
    1. Using identities to simplify the equation.
    2. Using known values of trigonometric ratios for special angles.
    3. Applying inverse trigonometric functions to solve for angles.

Applications of Trigonometry

  • Height and Distance Problems: Trigonometry is used to calculate the height of objects and distances between points using right-angled triangles.
  • Solving Triangles: Trigonometric ratios help in solving right-angled and non-right-angled triangles using the sine rule and cosine rule.

Sine and Cosine Rule

  • Sine Rule:
    a/sinA = b/sinB = c/sinC
    Used to solve any triangle (not necessarily right-angled).
  • Cosine Rule:
    c² = a² + b² − 2ab cosC
    Used to solve triangles when two sides and the included angle are known, or when all three sides are known.

Age Problems

Definition

  • Age problems are based on the relationships between the ages of two or more people, often over time, and require solving using algebra or arithmetic.

  • Age Ratio: Relationships between ages are given as ratios.

    • Five years ago: A’s age = x - 5, B’s age = y - 5. Hence, x - 5 = 3(y - 5) [Equation 1].
    • In 5 years: A’s age = x + 5, B’s age = y + 5. Hence, x + 5 = 2(y + 5) [Equation 2].

    Solving these two equations gives x = 35 and y = 15.

    Answer: A is 35 years old, and B is 15 years old.

Clock-Based Questions

Definition

  • Clock-based problems involve finding the position of the hour and minute hands of a clock at a given time or calculating angles between them.

Key Concepts

  • Angle Calculation: The clock has 360°, divided into 12 hours, so each hour represents 30°.
  • The minute hand moves 6° per minute (360° ÷ 60 = 6°).
  • The hour hand moves 0.5° per minute (30° ÷ 60 = 0.5°).

Examples:

  1. Clock-Based Problem 1: Finding the Angle Between Hands

    Problem: What is the angle between the hour and minute hands at 3:15?

    Solution:

    • The hour hand is at 90° (3 × 30° = 90°).
    • At 3:15, the hour hand moves 7.5° further (15 × 0.5°).
    • The minute hand is at 90° (15 × 6°).
    • The angle between them is: |(90° + 7.5°) - 90°| = 7.5°.

    Answer: 7.5°.

  2. Clock-Based Problem 2: When Do the Hands Overlap?

    Problem: When do the hour and minute hands overlap between 3:00 and 4:00?

    Solution:

    • The hour hand is at 90° at 3:00.
    • The minute hand moves faster than the hour hand.
    • The equation for overlap is: 90° + 0.5° × t = 6° × t.
    • Solve for t: 90° = 5.5° × t, so t = 90 ÷ 5.5 = 16.36 minutes.

    Answer: The hands overlap at 3:16:22.

  3. Clock-Based Problem 3: Time When Hands Are 90° Apart

    Problem: When are the hour and minute hands 90° apart between 3:00 and 4:00?

    Solution:

    • Use the formula: θ = |30H - 5.5M| and set θ = 90°.
    • Solve for M (minutes) for two different times within the hour when the hands form a 90° angle.
    • The two times when the hands are 90° apart are around 3:16:22 and 3:49:09.

    Answer: 3:16:22 and 3:49:09.

  • Set: A collection of well-defined objects. For example, A = {1, 2, 3} is a set containing the numbers 1, 2, and 3.
  • Element: An individual object in a set. For example, 1 is an element of the set A = {1, 2, 3}.
  • Subset: A set where all its elements are also contained in another set. For example, A = {1, 2} is a subset of B = {1, 2, 3}.
  • Union (A ∪ B): The set of all elements that belong to either set A, set B, or both.
  • Intersection (A ∩ B): The set of all elements that belong to both set A and set B.
  • Difference (A − B): The set of all elements that belong to A but not to B.
  • Complement (Aᶜ): The set of all elements not in A.
  • Universal Set (U): The set that contains all possible elements under consideration.

Example of Set Operations

  • A = {1, 2, 3} and B = {2, 3, 4}
  • Union: A ∪ B = {1, 2, 3, 4}
  • Intersection: A ∩ B = {2, 3}
  • Difference: A − B = {1}
  • Complement: If the universal set U = {1, 2, 3, 4, 5}, then Aᶜ = {4, 5}

Venn Diagrams

  • A Venn Diagram is a visual representation of sets and their relationships with each other.
  • It uses overlapping circles to show how sets relate to one another in terms of union, intersection, and difference.

Venn Diagram Components

  • Universal Set (U): Represented by a rectangle, it includes all possible elements under consideration.
  • Circles: Represent sets within the universal set. Overlapping circles show the intersection of sets.
  • Shaded Regions: Used to illustrate operations like union, intersection, and difference.

Operations in Venn Diagrams

  • Union: The area covered by both sets. If you have two sets A and B, the union is the entire area covered by both circles.
  • Intersection: The area where both circles overlap. It shows the common elements between two sets.
  • Difference: The area in one circle that does not overlap with the other circle. It shows the elements that belong to one set but not the other.
  • Complement: The area outside a circle represents the complement of the set. It includes all elements that do not belong to the set.

Types of Venn Diagrams

  1. Two-Set Venn Diagram

    • A two-set Venn diagram is used to represent the relationships between two sets.
    • Example: A = {1, 2, 3}, B = {2, 3, 4}
    • Union: A ∪ B = {1, 2, 3, 4}
    • Intersection: A ∩ B = {2, 3}

  2. Three-Set Venn Diagram

    • A three-set Venn diagram is used to represent the relationships between three sets.
    • Example: A = {1, 2, 3}, B = {2, 3, 4}, C = {3, 4, 5}
    • Union: A ∪ B ∪ C = {1, 2, 3, 4, 5}
    • Intersection: A ∩ B ∩ C = {3}

Examples of Venn Diagram Problems

Example 1:

In a survey, 80 people were asked if they liked tea or coffee. 50 liked tea, 40 liked coffee, and 30 liked both tea and coffee. How many liked only tea, only coffee, or both?

  • T = Tea lovers, C = Coffee lovers
  • ∣T∣ = 50 (Tea lovers)
  • ∣C∣ = 40 (Coffee lovers)
  • ∣T ∩ C∣ = 30 (People who like both)
  • Only Tea = ∣T∣ − ∣T ∩ C∣ = 50 − 30 = 20
  • Only Coffee = ∣C∣ − ∣T ∩ C∣ = 40 − 30 = 10
  • Thus, the final answers are:
    • 20 people like only tea.
    • 10 people like only coffee.
    • 30 people like both tea and coffee.

  • M = Students who like Mathematics, P = Students who like Physics
  • ∣M∣ = 40
  • ∣P∣ = 35
  • ∣M ∩ P∣ = 25
  • Only Mathematics = ∣M∣ − ∣M ∩ P∣ = 40 − 25 = 15
  • Only Physics = ∣P∣ − ∣M ∩ P∣ = 35 − 25 = 10
  • Thus, the number of students who like only one subject is:
    • 15 students like only Mathematics.

Definition

  • In logical deduction problems, you are provided with a set of conditions, and your task is to infer the correct relationships or conclusions from those conditions.

  • Example:
    • Premise 1: All men are mortal.

    • Conclusion: Socrates is mortal.

    • Syllogism problems typically involve two premises, and you need to deduce the correct conclusion based on the given facts.

    Example Problem:

    • Conclusion: Fido is an animal.
    • Answer: The conclusion is valid, as it follows from the premises.

  • These puzzles involve a set of conditions that describe relationships between different items or entities. You need to use these conditions to deduce certain facts or answer questions.

    Conditions:

    • A is taller than B.
    • C is taller than D.

    • C > D (C is taller than D).
    • B < D (B is shorter than D).

    • Answer: C is the tallest.

  • These problems involve determining someone's location, position, or direction based on a series of statements.

    Solution:

    • The person walks 10 meters North.
    • Then, he turns right (East) and walks 10 meters.
    • Finally, he turns right again (South) and walks 10 meters.
    • Since the total vertical displacement (North-South) is 0 (10 meters North and 10 meters South), the person is directly East of the starting point.

Statements and Assumptions

  • Logical deduction can also involve analyzing a statement and inferring whether the assumption based on it is true or false.

    • Example Problem:

    Solution:

Approaching Logical Deduction Problems

  • Read the problem carefully: Understand all the conditions or premises provided.
  • Break down the problem: Identify key facts and relationships that are mentioned.
  • Use process of elimination: In some cases, you can eliminate incorrect options and deduce the correct answer.

  • Consider all possibilities: When you’re unsure about a problem, list all possible scenarios and narrow them down based on the given conditions.

1. Basic Ratio & Proportion

Definition

  • Ratio and proportion are fundamental concepts in mathematics used to compare quantities and solve problems involving comparisons and distributions.

Ratio

  • A ratio is a way of comparing two or more quantities, showing the relative size of two values.
  • Formula: The ratio of two quantities a and b is written as:

    • Ratio = a/b or a : b

  • Example 1: If there are 3 apples and 2 oranges, the ratio of apples to oranges is:

    • Ratio = 3/2 or 3 : 2

  • Example 2: The ratio of boys to girls in a class is 4:5, meaning for every 4 boys, there are 5 girls in the class.

Types of Ratios

  1. Simple Ratio

    • When comparing two quantities. Example: The ratio of 5 to 2 is written as 5:2.

  2. Compound Ratio

    • A ratio that involves more than two quantities.
    • For example, the compound ratio of 2:3, 4:5, and 6:7 is:

      • Compound Ratio = (2/3) × (4/5) × (6/7) = 48/105 or 48 : 105

  3. Continued Ratio

    • A ratio involving three or more terms in a continued manner.
    • Example: If A:B = B:C, then A:B:C is the continued ratio.

Proportion

  • A proportion is an equation that expresses the equality of two ratios. It is typically written as:

    • a/b = c/d or a : b = c : d

  • This means the ratio of a to b is equal to the ratio of c to d.
  • Example: If 2/5 = 4/10, then the ratios are in proportion.

Properties of Proportions

  • Cross-multiplication: If a/b = c/d, then a × d = b × c.
  • Example: If 3/5 = x/9, we can solve for x using cross-multiplication:

    • 3 × x = 5 × 9 ⇒ 3x = 45 ⇒ x = 15

Solving Problems Involving Ratios and Proportions

  1. Example 1: A recipe requires 4 cups of flour and 3 cups of sugar. If you want to make a smaller batch using 2 cups of flour, how much sugar do you need?

    • The ratio of flour to sugar is 4:3. You need to find how much sugar is required for 2 cups of flour.

      • Set up the proportion: (4/3) = (2/x)

    • Cross-multiply: 4x = 6 ⇒ x = 1.5

      • Answer: You need 1.5 cups of sugar.

  2. Example 2: In a class, the ratio of boys to girls is 5:7. If there are 35 girls in the class, how many boys are there?

    • The ratio of boys to girls is 5:7. Let the number of boys be x.

      • Set up the proportion: (5/7) = (x/35)

    • Cross-multiply: 5 × 35 = 7 × x ⇒ 175 = 7x ⇒ x = 25

      • Answer: There are 25 boys in the class.

  3. Example 3: The total salary of A and B is $20,000. If the ratio of their salaries is 3:5, what is the salary of B?

    • Let the salary of A be 3x and the salary of B be 5x. The total salary is $20,000:

      • 3x + 5x = 20,000

    • 8x = 20,000 ⇒ x = 2,500

      • The salary of B is 5x = 5 × 2,500 = 12,500.

      Answer: The salary of B is $12,500.

Important Tips for Solving Ratio and Proportion Problems

  • Understand the ratio properly: Ensure that the quantities are being compared correctly.
  • Set up the proportion: Use the relation between the quantities to set up the proportion.
  • Cross-multiply: Helps in solving for the unknown quantity in the proportion.
  • Check your units: Ensure that quantities being compared are in the same unit before forming ratios.

2. Mixtures & Alligations

Definition

  • Mixtures and Alligations are mathematical techniques used to solve problems involving the mixing of two or more substances or quantities, often with different concentrations, values, or qualities.
  • These concepts are frequently used in competitive exams like JEE, NEET, and others.

Mixtures

  • A mixture is a combination of two or more substances in such a way that each substance retains its properties.

    • Types of Mixtures:

    • Homogeneous Mixture: A mixture where the components are uniformly distributed, and you cannot differentiate between them (e.g., salt dissolved in water).
    • Heterogeneous Mixture: A mixture where the components are not uniformly distributed, and you can see the individual substances (e.g., sand and salt mixture).

Alligation (Alligation Rule)

  • Alligation is used to solve problems where two or more substances (like solutions, liquids, or prices) are mixed to form a new mixture. It helps find the ratio in which the substances must be mixed or the mean value of the mixture.

    • Types of Alligation:

    • Alligation Alternate (AA): Used when you know the two concentrations or prices of two substances and want to find the ratio they should be mixed to achieve a desired concentration or price.

Steps for Alligation Alternate:

  1. Step 1: Write the concentration or price of the two components (substances) you are mixing.
  2. Step 2: Find the desired or mean concentration/price of the mixture.
  3. Step 3: Subtract the mean value from the concentration/price of each of the substances.
  4. Step 4: The differences you obtain are the parts in which the two substances must be mixed.

Formula for Alligation Alternate:

If two components with concentrations C₁ and C₂ are mixed to get a mixture with concentration Cₘ, the ratio of mixing is:

Ratio = (C₂ - Cₘ) : (Cₘ - C₁)

Alligation Mean (AM)

  • Alligation Mean is used when the mean (average) value of a mixture is required, based on the concentrations and the quantities of each substance.

Formula for Alligation Mean:

Mean Value = (C₁ × W₁) + (C₂ × W₂) + ... + (Cₙ × Wₙ) / (W₁ + W₂ + ... + Wₙ)

Where:

  • C₁, C₂, ..., Cₙ are the concentrations or prices of the substances.
  • W₁, W₂, ..., Wₙ are the quantities or weights of the substances.

Examples of Mixtures & Alligations Problems

  1. Example 1: Alligation Alternate

    Problem: A chemist mixes two solutions of acid. One contains 30% acid, and the other contains 70% acid. How much of each solution should be mixed to obtain a 50% acid solution?

    Solution:

    1. Step 1: Write the concentrations:
      • First solution = 30% acid
      • Second solution = 70% acid
      • Desired solution = 50% acid
    2. Step 2: Subtract the mean concentration from each of the components:

      3. 70 - 50 = 20 and 50 - 30 = 20

    3. The ratio in which the solutions should be mixed is 20:20, or 1:1.

    Answer: Mix the two solutions in a 1:1 ratio.

3. Weighted Averages & Mean

Mean (Arithmetic Mean)

  • The mean (or average) of a set of numbers is calculated by summing all the numbers and then dividing the sum by the total number of values in the set.

Formula for Arithmetic Mean:

Mean = (Sum of all values) / (Number of values)

For example, if the numbers are 3, 5, 7, and 9:

Mean = (3 + 5 + 7 + 9) / 4 = 24 / 4 = 6

Weighted Average

  • A weighted average is used when some values in a dataset are more important or have more significance than others. Instead of each value contributing equally to the average, each value is multiplied by a weight that reflects its importance.

Formula for Weighted Average:

Weighted Average = (x₁ × w₁) + (x₂ × w₂) + ... + (xₙ × wₙ) / (w₁ + w₂ + ... + wₙ)

Where:

  • x₁, x₂, ..., xₙ are the values.
  • w₁, w₂, ..., wₙ are the corresponding weights.

The sum of the weights is the total weight, and the formula computes the average considering these weights.

Examples of Weighted Averages

  1. Example 1: Simple Weighted Average

    Problem: A student has the following grades for different subjects:

    • Math: 80 (weight 2)
    • Science: 90 (weight 3)
    • English: 85 (weight 1)

    Find the weighted average of the grades.

    Solution: Using the formula for weighted average:

    Weighted Average = (80 × 2 + 90 × 3 + 85 × 1) / (2 + 3 + 1)

    Weighted Average = (160 + 270 + 85) / 6 = 515 / 6 ≈ 85.83

    Answer: The weighted average grade is approximately 85.83.

  2. Example 2: Weighted Average in Economics

    Problem: A company produces two types of products, A and B. The sales of product A are 300 units at ₹20 each, and the sales of product B are 500 units at ₹15 each. Find the weighted average price of the products sold.

    Solution: Using the weighted average formula:

    Price of A = ₹20, Quantity of A = 300

    Price of B = ₹15, Quantity of B = 500

    Weighted Average Price = (20 × 300 + 15 × 500) / (300 + 500)

    Weighted Average Price = (6000 + 7500) / 800 = 13500 / 800 = ₹16.875

    Answer: The weighted average price of the products sold is ₹16.875.

Key Differences Between Mean and Weighted Average

  • Mean (Arithmetic Mean): Every value contributes equally to the average.
  • Weighted Average: Each value has a different level of importance or weight, and these weights affect the final average.

Applications of Weighted Averages

  • Grades and Scores: When calculating the average grade for a student, where each subject or exam has a different weight.
  • Financial Analysis: Weighted averages are used to calculate the average cost of capital, average portfolio returns, and stock price indices.
  • Economics: Weighted averages help calculate the average price in an economy or for specific goods with varying importance.

4. Partnership & Investments

Partnership

In a partnership, two or more individuals or entities come together to run a business, share profits, and bear the losses according to a mutually agreed ratio. The agreement usually specifies how profits, losses, and capital will be divided among the partners.

Key Concepts in Partnership

  • Capital Contribution: This refers to the amount of money or assets each partner invests in the business. The partnership ratio (profit-sharing ratio) is often based on the amount of capital invested.
  • Profit Sharing Ratio: The ratio in which the partners will share the profits (or losses) of the business. This can be based on their capital contribution or some other agreement.
  • Time Factor: If the partners invest their capital for different periods, the time factor is considered while calculating the profit-sharing ratio. The effective contribution of each partner is their capital multiplied by the time for which it is invested.

Formula for Partnership

If the capital contributions of two partners P₁ and P₂ for different time periods T₁ and T₂ are known, then the share of profits can be calculated using the following formula:

Profit of Partner 1 = (P₁ × T₁) / (P₁ × T₁ + P₂ × T₂) × Total Profit

Profit of Partner 2 = (P₂ × T₂) / (P₁ × T₁ + P₂ × T₂) × Total Profit

Where:

  • P₁ and P₂ are the amounts of capital invested by Partner 1 and Partner 2, respectively.
  • T₁ and T₂ are the time periods for which the capital is invested.
  • Total Profit is the total amount of profit or loss made by the business.

Types of Partnership

  • Fixed Capital Partnership: In this type of partnership, the capital contribution of the partners remains constant throughout the business period.
  • Fluctuating Capital Partnership: Here, the capital contribution of the partners may change during the course of the business, i.e., the partners can add or withdraw capital as per their needs.

Investments

An investment involves putting money or capital into a business or asset with the expectation of receiving a return or profit over time. In partnerships, the initial investment and any subsequent additions to it determine the profit-sharing ratio among the partners.

Key Terms in Investments

  • Principal Amount (P): The initial investment made by a partner.
  • Rate of Interest (R): The percentage of return on the invested amount, usually on an annual basis.
  • Time Period (T): The duration for which the investment is made.

Simple Interest (SI)

The interest earned on the principal over a period of time, calculated using the formula:

SI = (P × R × T) / 100

Where:

  • P = Principal amount
  • R = Rate of interest per annum
  • T = Time in years

Compound Interest (CI)

Interest that is calculated on both the principal and the accumulated interest. The formula for compound interest is:

A = P(1 + R / 100)^T

Where:

  • A = Amount after interest
  • P = Principal amount
  • R = Rate of interest per annum
  • T = Time in years

    Solution: Since both partners invested the same amount, the profit will be shared equally.

Chapter 12 - Banking & Financial Mathematics

1. Simple & Compound Interest Advanced Problems

i. Simple Interest (SI)

Formula for Simple Interest:

SI = P × R × T / 100

Where:

  • P = Principal amount
  • R = Rate of interest per annum
  • T = Time in years

The total amount A after T years is given by:

A = P + SI = P(1 + R × T / 100)

ii. Compound Interest (CI)

Formula for Compound Interest:

A = P(1 + R / 100) ^ T

Where:

  • P = Principal amount
  • R = Rate of interest per annum
  • T = Time in years
  • A = Total amount after T years

To find the compound interest, we subtract the principal P from the amount A:

CI = A - P = P(1 + R / 100) ^ T - P

For compound interest compounded more frequently (quarterly, monthly), the formula is adjusted to account for the frequency of compounding.

iii. Advanced Problems: Simple Interest

Problem 1: Find the Time Period for Simple Interest

Problem: A sum of ₹1,500 is invested at 8% per annum simple interest. After 3 years, the interest amount is ₹360. Find the principal.

Solution: Using the formula for Simple Interest:

SI = P × R × T / 100

We know:

  • SI = ₹360
  • R = 8%
  • T = 3 years

Substituting the values:

360 = P × 8 × 3 / 100

360 = 24P / 100

P = (360 × 100) / 24 = ₹1,500

Answer: The principal is ₹1,500.

Problem 2: Find the Interest Earned and Total Amount

Problem: A sum of ₹12,000 is invested for 2 years at a rate of 10% per annum simple interest. Find the total amount at the end of 2 years.

Solution: Using the formula for Simple Interest:

SI = P × R × T / 100

Substitute the values:

  • P = ₹12,000
  • R = 10%
  • T = 2 years

SI = 12,000 × 10 × 2 / 100 = ₹2,400

The total amount A will be:

A = P + SI = ₹12,000 + ₹2,400 = ₹14,400

Answer: The total amount after 2 years is ₹14,400.

iv. Advanced Problems: Compound Interest

Problem 1: Compound Interest with Quarterly Compounding

Problem: A sum of ₹10,000 is invested at 8% per annum interest, compounded quarterly for 2 years. Find the compound interest and the total amount.

Solution: The formula for Compound Interest when interest is compounded quarterly is:

A = P(1 + R / (4 × 100)) ^ (4 × T)

Where:

  • P = ₹10,000
  • R = 8%
  • T = 2 years

Substitute the values:

A = 10,000 × (1 + 8 / (4 × 100)) ^ (4 × 2)

A = 10,000 × (1 + 2 / 100) ^ 8 = 10,000 × (1.02) ^ 8

A ≈ 10,000 × 1.3728 = ₹13,728

Now, to find the Compound Interest:

CI = A - P = ₹13,728 - ₹10,000 = ₹3,728

Answer: The compound interest is ₹3,728, and the total amount is ₹13,728.

2. Inflation & Deflation Calculations

Inflation Calculation

Inflation is generally measured as the percentage change in the Consumer Price Index (CPI) over a specific period of time (usually annually).

The formula to calculate inflation is:

Inflation Rate = (CPI at End of Year - CPI at Beginning of Year) / CPI at Beginning of Year × 100

Where:

  • CPI (Consumer Price Index) is a measure that examines the weighted average of prices of a basket of consumer goods and services.

Example 1: Inflation Calculation

Problem: The Consumer Price Index (CPI) in January was 120 and in December it was 130. What is the inflation rate for the year?

Solution: Using the formula for inflation:

Inflation Rate = (130 - 120) / 120 × 100

Inflation Rate = 10 / 120 × 100 = 8.33%

Answer: The inflation rate is 8.33%.

3. Stocks, Shares & Debentures

Shares and Stocks

Shares and stocks are similar concepts, but they have subtle differences.

  • Share: A share represents a unit of ownership in a company. When you buy a share, you own a portion of the company and are entitled to a share of its profits (dividends) and voting rights at general meetings.
  • Stock: Stock refers to the collective ownership in a company, which consists of multiple shares. It’s often used to refer to ownership in a company without specifying the number of shares.

Types of Shares

  • Equity Shares (Common Shares):
    • Represent ownership in a company.
    • Carry voting rights and entitle the shareholder to dividends.
    • Dividends depend on the company’s profit.
    • Risk is higher, as equity shareholders are paid after debenture holders and preference shareholders in case of liquidation.
  • Preference Shares:
    • Provide a fixed dividend before equity shareholders receive any.
    • Generally, preference shareholders do not have voting rights.
    • In case of liquidation, preference shareholders are paid before equity shareholders.

Key Features of Shares:

  • Face Value: The nominal or original value of a share, typically set by the company.
  • Market Value: The price at which shares are traded in the stock market.
  • Dividend: A share of the company's profits paid to shareholders.
  • Capital Gain: Profit made by selling shares at a higher price than the purchase price.

Debentures

A debenture is a debt instrument issued by companies to raise funds. It represents a loan taken by the company from debenture holders. Unlike shares, debentures do not offer ownership but instead offer fixed interest payments to the debenture holders.

Key Features of Debentures:

  • Debt Instrument: Debentures are a form of borrowing for the company, and they are not ownership in the company.
  • Fixed Interest: Debenture holders receive fixed interest, also known as coupon rates, regularly.

Secured and Unsecured Debentures:

  • Secured Debentures: Backed by the company’s assets as collateral.
  • Unsecured Debentures: Not backed by any specific assets, and hence, considered riskier.

Types of Debentures:

  • Convertible Debentures: Can be converted into a specified number of equity shares after a certain period.
  • Non-Convertible Debentures: Cannot be converted into shares but offer higher interest rates due to the lack of convertibility.
  • Redeemable Debentures: Can be redeemed after a certain period.
  • Irredeemable Debentures: Cannot be redeemed but continue to pay interest.

Comparison: Shares vs. Debentures

Aspect Shares Debentures
Nature Ownership in the company Loan to the company
Returns Dividends, which depend on profit Fixed interest (coupon rate)
Risk Higher risk, as returns depend on company performance Lower risk compared to shares (especially if secured)
Voting Rights Yes, shareholders can vote in company matters No voting rights
Priority in Liquidation Last to be paid (after debt holders) First to be paid (before equity holders)
Convertibility Cannot be converted into other instruments Convertible into equity shares (in the case of convertible debentures)
Repayment No fixed repayment, as it’s ownership-based Must be repaid at maturity or as per terms

Financial Terms Related to Shares and Debentures

Stock Market Terms:

  • Face Value: The nominal value of a share or debenture, set by the issuing company.
  • Market Value: The price at which shares/debentures are bought or sold in the market.
  • Dividend Yield: The annual dividend paid by a company divided by the market price of the share.
  • Capital Gain: Profit from selling shares at a higher price than their purchase price.
  • Coupon Rate (for Debentures): The interest rate paid by the issuer on the debenture’s face value.

Debenture Terms:

  • Redemption Period: The time after which the debenture principal must be repaid.
  • Interest/ Coupon Payment: The fixed interest paid by the company to debenture holders.

Example Problems

Problem 1: Calculation of Dividend for Equity Shares

Problem: A company has issued 1000 equity shares of face value ₹100 each. The company declares a 10% dividend. What is the total dividend earned by a shareholder with 50 shares?

Solution:

  • Dividend per share = 10% of ₹100 = ₹10
  • Total Dividend = 50 shares × ₹10 = ₹500

Answer: The shareholder will earn ₹500 as dividend.
