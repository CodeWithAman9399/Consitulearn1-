Chapter 1 - Structure of Atom

1. Atomic Models

Dalton’s Atomic Model (1803) – Indivisible Atoms

  • Proposed by: John Dalton
  • Key Features:
    • Atoms are tiny, indivisible particles.
    • Atoms of the same element are identical in mass and properties.
    • Atoms combine in simple whole-number ratios to form compounds.
    • Atoms cannot be created or destroyed (Law of Conservation of Mass).
  • Limitations:
    • Did not explain the internal structure of atoms.
    • Could not explain the discovery of subatomic particles.

Thomson’s Plum Pudding Model (1897) – Discovery of Electrons

  • Proposed by: J.J. Thomson
  • Key Features:
    • Atom is a positively charged sphere with negatively charged electrons embedded in it.
    • Explained the existence of electrons (discovered in cathode ray experiments).
    • Atoms are electrically neutral.
  • Limitations:
    • Could not explain atomic stability.
    • Did not account for the presence of a nucleus.

Rutherford’s Nuclear Model (1911) – Discovery of the Nucleus

  • Proposed by: Ernest Rutherford
  • Experiment: Gold Foil Experiment
  • Key Features:
    • Atom has a dense, positively charged nucleus at the center.
    • Electrons move around the nucleus in empty space.
    • Most of the atom is empty space.
  • Limitations:
    • Could not explain why electrons do not fall into the nucleus.
    • Did not explain atomic spectra.

Bohr’s Atomic Model (1913) – Quantized Orbits

  • Proposed by: Niels Bohr
  • Key Features:
    • Electrons revolve in discrete orbits (energy levels).
    • Energy is quantized – Electrons can only exist in specific energy levels.
    • Electrons absorb or emit energy only when jumping between orbits.
  • Bohr’s Energy Equation: E n = -13.6 n 2 eV
  • Limitations:
    • Could not explain spectra of multi-electron atoms.
    • Did not account for electron wave nature.

Quantum Mechanical Model (1926) – Electron Clouds

  • Proposed by: Schrödinger, Heisenberg, and de Broglie
  • Key Features:
    • Electrons are not in fixed orbits, but exist in probability clouds (orbitals).
    • Schrödinger’s Wave Equation describes electron behavior.
    • Heisenberg’s Uncertainty Principle: It is impossible to know both position and momentum of an electron simultaneously.
  • Types of Orbitals:
    • s-orbital (spherical)
    • p-orbital (dumbbell-shaped)
    • d & f orbitals (complex shapes)
  • Most Accurate Model used today in quantum mechanics!

2. Electronic Configuration

Electronic configuration refers to the systematic arrangement of electrons in different energy levels (shells and subshells) of an atom.

1. Quantum Numbers and Orbitals

  • Principal Quantum Number (n): Indicates the main energy level (shell).
  • Azimuthal Quantum Number (l): Represents the shape of the orbital.
  • Magnetic Quantum Number (mₗ): Specifies the orientation of orbitals.
  • Spin Quantum Number (mₛ): Describes the spin of an electron.

2. Rules for Writing Electronic Configuration

  • Aufbau Principle: Electrons fill orbitals in order of increasing energy levels.
  • Pauli’s Exclusion Principle: No two electrons can have the same set of four quantum numbers.
  • Hund’s Rule: Each orbital in a subshell is singly occupied before pairing.

3. Notation for Electronic Configuration

Example: Carbon (C) → 1s² 2s² 2p²

4. Electronic Configuration of Elements

Element Atomic Number Electronic Configuration

Helium (He) 2 1s²
Carbon (C) 6 1s² 2s² 2p²
Neon (Ne) 10 1s² 2s² 2p⁶
Sodium (Na) 11 1s² 2s² 2p⁶ 3s¹

5. Exceptional Electronic Configurations

Certain elements deviate from the Aufbau principle due to stability factors:

  • Chromium (Cr): [Ar] 4s¹ 3d⁵ instead of [Ar] 4s² 3d⁴
  • Copper (Cu): [Ar] 4s¹ 3d¹⁰ instead of [Ar] 4s² 3d⁹

6. Importance of Electronic Configuration

  • Determines chemical properties and reactivity.
  • Explains bonding, magnetism, and conductivity.
  • Helps predict ion formation.

Quantum Numbers

  • Represents the main energy level (shell) of an electron.
  • Determines the size and energy of the orbital.
  • Possible values: n = 1, 2, 3, 4, ...

Azimuthal Quantum Number (l)

  • Represents the subshell (shape of the orbital).
  • Values: l = 0 to (n-1)
  • Shapes:
    • l = 0 → s-orbital (Spherical)
    • l = 1 → p-orbital (Dumbbell)
    • l = 2 → d-orbital (Cloverleaf)
    • l = 3 → f-orbital (Complex)

Magnetic Quantum Number (mₗ)

  • Determines the orientation of an orbital in space.
  • Values: mₗ = -l to +l

Spin Quantum Number (mₛ)

  • Represents the spin direction of an electron.
  • Possible values: +½ or −½

Chapter 2 - States of Matter

1. Gases & Gas Laws

Introduction to Gases

  • Gases are one of the fundamental states of matter, characterized by:
    • No fixed shape or fixed volume.
    • Particles that are far apart and move freely.
    • Compressibility and ability to expand.

Properties of Gases

  • Mass: Gases have mass, but they are much less dense than solids or liquids.
  • Volume: Gases expand to fill the entire container.
  • Pressure (P): Force exerted by gas molecules per unit area. Measured in atm, mmHg, or Pascal (Pa).
  • Temperature (T): Affects the kinetic energy of gas molecules. Measured in Kelvin (K).
  • Density (ρ): Defined as mass/volume (ρ = m/V).

Gas Laws

  1. Boyle’s Law (Pressure-Volume Relationship)

    • The volume of a gas is inversely proportional to its pressure at constant temperature.
    • Formula: P₁V₁ = P₂V₂
    • Graph: A hyperbolic curve (as pressure increases, volume decreases).
    • Example: Squeezing a balloon reduces its volume.

  2. Charles’ Law (Temperature-Volume Relationship)

    • The volume of a gas is directly proportional to its absolute temperature (K) at constant pressure.
    • Formula: V₁/T₁ = V₂/T₂
    • Graph: A straight line (as temperature increases, volume increases).
    • Example: A balloon expands when heated.

  3. Gay-Lussac’s Law (Pressure-Temperature Relationship)

    • The pressure of a gas is directly proportional to its absolute temperature at constant volume.
    • Formula: P₁/T₁ = P₂/T₂
    • Graph: A straight line (as temperature increases, pressure increases).
    • Example: A sealed aerosol can may burst when heated.

  4. Avogadro’s Law (Volume-Number of Molecules Relationship)

    • The volume of a gas is directly proportional to the number of gas molecules at constant pressure and temperature.
    • Formula: V₁/n₁ = V₂/n₂
    • Example: Adding more gas to a balloon increases its size.

  5. Ideal Gas Law

    • Combining all gas laws:
    • Formula: PV = nRT
    • Where:
      • P = Pressure (atm or Pa)
      • V = Volume (L or m³)
      • n = Number of moles
      • R = Gas constant (0.0821 L·atm/mol·K)
      • T = Temperature (K)
    • Example: Used to calculate unknown gas properties in experiments.

Dalton’s Law of Partial Pressures

  • The total pressure of a gas mixture is equal to the sum of the partial pressures of individual gases.
  • Formula: Ptotal = P₁ + P₂ + P₃ + …
  • Example: The pressure of oxygen and nitrogen in air contributes to atmospheric pressure.

Graham’s Law of Diffusion & Effusion

  • Diffusion: Spreading of gas molecules from high to low concentration.
  • Effusion: Movement of gas through a small hole without collisions.
  • Law: The rate of diffusion/effusion is inversely proportional to the square root of its molar mass.
  • Formula: r₁/r₂ = √(M₂/M₁)
  • Example: Lighter gases (like hydrogen) diffuse faster than heavier gases (like oxygen).

2. Liquids & Solids

Introduction

Liquids and solids are two of the fundamental states of matter. They differ in particle arrangement, movement, and intermolecular forces.

Property Solids Liquids
Shape Fixed Takes shape of container
Volume Fixed Fixed
Particle Arrangement Tightly packed Loosely packed
Intermolecular Forces Strongest Moderate
Compressibility Negligible Slightly compressible
Fluidity Does not flow Flows easily

Liquids

Properties of Liquids

  • Definite volume but no fixed shape (takes the shape of the container).
  • Fluidity (ability to flow).
  • Surface tension (due to cohesive forces between molecules).
  • Viscosity (resistance to flow, e.g., honey is more viscous than water).
  • Capillary action (rise of liquid in narrow tubes due to adhesive and cohesive forces).

Intermolecular Forces in Liquids

  • Dipole-Dipole Forces: Present in polar molecules (e.g., H₂O, HCl).
  • Hydrogen Bonding: Strong force in molecules with H-F, H-O, H-N bonds (e.g., water).
  • Van der Waals Forces (London Dispersion Forces): Weak forces in non-polar molecules (e.g., O₂, N₂).

Vaporization & Boiling Point

  • Vaporization: Liquid to gas conversion.
  • Boiling Point: Temperature at which vapor pressure equals atmospheric pressure.
  • Factors Affecting Boiling Point:
    • Stronger intermolecular forces → Higher boiling point.
    • Higher altitude → Lower boiling point.

Solids

Properties of Solids

  • Definite shape and volume.
  • High density and incompressibility.
  • Strong intermolecular forces keep particles fixed in position.
  • Rigid structure with minimal particle movement (vibrations).

Types of Solids

Solids are classified into crystalline and amorphous solids.

Property Crystalline Solids Amorphous Solids
Particle Arrangement Regular, ordered Irregular, disordered
Melting Point Sharp melting point Melts over a range
Examples Salt (NaCl), Diamond, Quartz Glass, Rubber, Plastic

Types of Crystalline Solids

Type Particles Bonding Example
Ionic Solids Ions Ionic bonds NaCl, KCl
Covalent (Network) Solids Atoms Covalent bonds Diamond, Graphite, SiO₂
Metallic Solids Metal atoms Metallic bonds Fe, Cu, Al
Molecular Solids Molecules Van der Waals forces Ice (H₂O), CO₂ (Dry Ice)

Phase Changes Between States

  • Melting: Solid → Liquid
  • Freezing: Liquid → Solid
  • Vaporization: Liquid → Gas
  • Condensation: Gas → Liquid
  • Sublimation: Solid → Gas (e.g., Dry Ice)
  • Deposition: Gas → Solid

3. Phase Transitions

Introduction

  • Phase transitions are physical changes where a substance changes its state (solid, liquid, gas) due to temperature and pressure variations.
  • These changes are reversible and involve energy exchange in the form of heat.

Types of Phase Transitions

Phase Change From → To Energy Change Example
Melting Solid → Liquid Absorbs heat Ice melting into water
Freezing Liquid → Solid Releases heat Water freezing into ice
Vaporization Liquid → Gas Absorbs heat Water boiling into steam
Condensation Gas → Liquid Releases heat Steam turning into water droplets
Sublimation Solid → Gas Absorbs heat Dry ice turning into CO₂ gas
Deposition Gas → Solid Releases heat Frost forming on a cold surface

Explanation of Each Phase Transition

  • Melting (Fusion)
    • A solid absorbs heat energy, breaking intermolecular forces, and transitions to a liquid.
    • Occurs at melting point (e.g., Ice melts at 0°C).
  • Freezing (Solidification)
    • A liquid loses heat energy, forming stronger intermolecular forces, and turns into a solid.
    • Occurs at freezing point (same as melting point for pure substances).
  • Vaporization (Boiling & Evaporation)
    • Boiling: Rapid vaporization at boiling point (100°C for water at 1 atm).
    • Evaporation: Slow vaporization at any temperature below boiling point.
  • Condensation
    • Gas loses heat and turns into a liquid (reverse of vaporization).
    • Example: Water droplets forming on a cold glass.
  • Sublimation
    • A solid directly changes into a gas without becoming a liquid.
    • Example: Dry ice (solid CO₂) sublimates at room temperature.
  • Deposition
    • A gas directly turns into a solid without passing through the liquid phase.
    • Example: Frost formation on a window.

Energy Changes in Phase Transitions

  • Endothermic (Absorbs Heat): Melting, Vaporization, Sublimation.
  • Exothermic (Releases Heat): Freezing, Condensation, Deposition.

Latent Heat

  • Latent Heat of Fusion (ΔHfus): Heat required to convert solid → liquid.
  • Latent Heat of Vaporization (ΔHvap): Heat required to convert liquid → gas.

Phase Diagram

A phase diagram shows the relationship between temperature, pressure, and phase changes.

Key Points on a Phase Diagram:

  • Triple Point: The temperature & pressure where solid, liquid, and gas coexist in equilibrium.
  • Critical Point: The temperature & pressure above which the gas and liquid phase become indistinguishable (supercritical fluid).
Chapter 3 - Thermodynamics

1. Laws of Thermodynamics

Introduction

  • The Laws of Thermodynamics describe the principles governing energy transfer, heat, and work in a system.
  • These laws are fundamental in physics, chemistry, and engineering.

Zeroth Law of Thermodynamics (Thermal Equilibrium)

  • Statement: If two systems (A and B) are each in thermal equilibrium with a third system (C), then A and B are also in thermal equilibrium with each other.
  • Implication:
    • Defines temperature as a fundamental property.
    • Basis for thermometers (temperature measurement).
  • Example: If a hot metal rod is placed in water, both eventually reach the same temperature.

First Law of Thermodynamics (Law of Energy Conservation)

  • Statement: Energy cannot be created or destroyed, only transferred or converted from one form to another.
  • Mathematical Expression: ΔU = Q - W

    where:

    • ΔU = Change in internal energy
    • Q = Heat added to the system
    • W = Work done by the system
  • Implication:
    • Heat can be converted into work and vice versa.
    • Energy remains constant in an isolated system.
  • Example: In a steam engine, heat energy is converted into mechanical work.

Second Law of Thermodynamics (Entropy & Direction of Heat Flow)

  • Statement: Entropy (disorder) of an isolated system always increases over time.
  • Implications:
    • Heat always flows from a hotter body to a colder body, not the reverse.
    • No engine can be 100% efficient (some energy is always lost as heat).
  • Mathematical Expression (Entropy Change): ΔS ≥ 0

    where ΔS is entropy change.

  • Examples:
    • Ice melts into water, increasing entropy.
    • Heat dissipates from hot coffee to the surrounding air.

Third Law of Thermodynamics (Absolute Zero & Entropy at 0K)

  • Statement: At absolute zero (0 K or -273.15°C), the entropy of a perfect crystal is exactly zero.
  • Implications:
    • Absolute zero is the lowest possible temperature, but it is unattainable in practice.
    • As temperature decreases, molecular motion decreases, reducing entropy.
  • Example: Near absolute zero, gases condense into liquids or solids, showing minimal motion.

2. Enthalpy, Entropy & Gibbs Free Energy

Introduction

These thermodynamic properties help describe the energy changes, spontaneity, and disorder of chemical and physical processes.

  • Enthalpy (H) → Heat content of a system.
  • Entropy (S) → Measure of disorder or randomness.
  • Gibbs Free Energy (G) → Determines whether a reaction occurs spontaneously.

Enthalpy (H) – Heat Content of a System

Definition:

The total heat energy of a system at constant pressure.

Mathematical Expression:

ΔH = Hfinal − Hinitial

Types of Enthalpy Changes:

  • ΔH > 0 (Endothermic reaction) → Absorbs heat (e.g., melting ice).
  • ΔH < 0 (Exothermic reaction) → Releases heat (e.g., burning fuel).

Examples of Enthalpy Change:

  • Combustion: Exothermic (Releases heat)
  • Evaporation: Endothermic (Absorbs heat)

Entropy (S) – Measure of Disorder

Definition:

Measures randomness or disorder in a system.

Mathematical Expression:

ΔS = Sfinal − Sinitial

Key Concept:

  • Greater disorder → Higher entropy
  • Ordered state → Lower entropy

Entropy & Spontaneity:

  • ΔS > 0: More disorder (e.g., ice melting).
  • ΔS < 0: Less disorder (e.g., gas condensing into liquid).

Examples of Entropy Change:

  • Ice melting → Increases entropy (ΔS > 0).
  • Water freezing → Decreases entropy (ΔS < 0).

Gibbs Free Energy (G) – Predicting Spontaneity

Definition:

Determines if a reaction occurs spontaneously or requires external energy.

Mathematical Expression:

ΔG = ΔH − TΔS

Where:

  • ΔG = Gibbs free energy change
  • ΔH = Enthalpy change
  • T = Temperature in Kelvin
  • ΔS = Entropy change

Spontaneity of a Reaction:

ΔG Value Spontaneity Example
ΔG < 0 Spontaneous Ice melting at room temperature
ΔG > 0 Non-Spontaneous Water freezing at room temperature
ΔG = 0 Equilibrium Phase equilibrium (e.g., boiling water at 100°C)

3. Equilibrium Concepts

Introduction

  • Equilibrium is a state where the rate of the forward reaction equals the rate of the reverse reaction.
  • Concentrations of reactants and products remain constant over time.

Types of Equilibrium

Physical Equilibrium

  • Involves phase transitions where rates of change are equal.
  • Examples:
    • Solid-Liquid Equilibrium: H2O(s) ⇌ H2O(l) (melting/freezing at 0°C).
    • Liquid-Gas Equilibrium: H2O(l) ⇌ H2O(g) (evaporation/condensation at boiling point).

Chemical Equilibrium

  • Occurs when the rate of the forward reaction equals the rate of the reverse reaction.
  • Example: N2 + 3H2 ⇌ 2NH3.
  • Dynamic in nature: Reactions continue microscopically, but macroscopic properties remain constant.

Law of Chemical Equilibrium & Equilibrium Constant

Law of Mass Action

For a reaction: aA + bB ⇌ cC + dD

Equilibrium constant (K) is given by:

K = ([C]c [D]d) / ([A]a [B]b)

Types of Equilibrium Constants

  • Kc: Concentration-based, using molar concentrations (mol/L).
  • Kp: Pressure-based, used for gaseous reactions.

Relation: Kp = Kc (RT)Δn, where Δn = (moles of gaseous products - moles of gaseous reactants).

Le Chatelier’s Principle

If a system at equilibrium is subjected to a change, the equilibrium shifts to oppose the change.

Factor Change Applied Equilibrium Shift
Concentration Increase reactants Forward direction (more products)
Increase products Reverse direction (more reactants)
Pressure Increase pressure Towards fewer gas molecules
Decrease pressure Towards more gas molecules
Temperature Increase temperature Endothermic direction
Decrease temperature Exothermic direction
Catalyst Added No shift, only speeds up equilibrium

Gibbs Free Energy & Equilibrium

Relation: ΔG = -RT lnK

  • ΔG < 0: Spontaneous reaction, K > 1 (more products).
  • ΔG > 0: Non-spontaneous reaction, K < 1 (more reactants).
  • ΔG = 0: System is at equilibrium, K = 1.

Summary

  • Equilibrium involves a balance between forward and reverse reactions.
  • Law of Mass Action defines equilibrium constant (K).
  • Le Chatelier’s Principle explains shifts due to external changes.
  • Gibbs Free Energy determines spontaneity and equilibrium position.
Chapter 4 - Chemical Kinametics

1. Rate Laws

Introduction

  • The Rate Law expresses how the rate of a chemical reaction depends on the concentration of reactants.
  • It helps in determining the reaction mechanism and predicting reaction behavior.

General Form of Rate Law

  • For a reaction: aA + bB → cC + dD
  • The rate law is given by:

    Rate = k[A]m[B]n

  • Where:
    • k = Rate constant (depends on temperature).
    • [A], [B] = Concentrations of reactants.
    • m, n = Order of reaction with respect to A and B (determined experimentally).
    • Overall Order = m + n.

Types of Rate Laws

  1. Differential Rate Law

    • Shows how rate depends on reactant concentrations.
    • Example: For a first-order reaction → Rate = k[A]

  2. Integrated Rate Law

    • Relates concentration to time.
    • Useful for determining half-life and predicting concentration changes over time.

Reaction Order & Rate Laws

Order Rate Law Half-Life Formula
Zero Order Rate = k t1/2 = [A]0 / 2k
First Order Rate = k[A] t1/2 = 0.693 / k
Second Order Rate = k[A]2 t1/2 = 1 / k[A]0

Half-Life (t1/2)

  • Half-life is the time taken for a reactant's concentration to reduce to half its initial value.
  • First-order reactions have a constant half-life.
  • Zero and second-order reactions have variable half-life depending on initial concentration.

Factors Affecting Reaction Rate

  • Concentration: Higher concentration → Faster reaction.
  • Temperature: Higher temperature → Increases rate due to more collisions.
  • Catalyst: Lowers activation energy → Speeds up reaction.
  • Surface Area: More surface area → Faster reaction.

2. Reaction Mechanisms

Introduction

  • A reaction mechanism is the step-by-step sequence of elementary reactions by which an overall chemical reaction occurs.
  • It describes how reactants are converted into products, including the formation of intermediates and the breaking/forming of bonds.

Elementary Reactions & Molecularity

Molecularity Definition Example
Unimolecular Involves a single reactant molecule A → B
Bimolecular Involves two reactant molecules A + B → C
Termolecular Involves three reactant molecules A + B + C → D (Rare)

Reaction Intermediates & Transition States

  • Intermediates: Species that are formed in one step and consumed in another.
  • Transition State: High-energy, unstable configuration of molecules during bond breaking/forming.

Example Mechanism:

For the reaction: 2NO2 → 2NO + O2

Possible mechanism:

  • NO2 + NO2 → NO3 + NO (Slow, Rate-Determining Step)
  • NO3 → NO + O2 (Fast)

NO3 is an intermediate.

Rate-Determining Step (RDS)

  • The slowest step in a reaction mechanism.
  • Overall reaction rate depends on this step.
  • Rate law is determined by the reactants in the RDS.

Example:

For: A + B → C + D

Mechanism:

  • A + B → AB* (Slow, RDS)
  • AB* → C + D (Fast)

Since step 1 is slow, rate law depends only on A & B:

Rate = k[A][B]

Catalysts & Reaction Mechanisms

  • Catalysts provide an alternative pathway with lower activation energy.
  • They are not consumed in the reaction.

Example:

Enzymes in biological reactions.

3. Factors Influencing Reaction Rate

Introduction

  • The rate of a chemical reaction refers to how fast reactants are converted into products.
  • Several factors influence this rate by affecting the frequency and energy of molecular collisions.

Factors Affecting Reaction Rate

  1. Concentration of Reactants

    • Higher concentration → Faster reaction (more collisions).
    • Example: A concentrated acid reacts faster than a dilute acid with the same reactant.
    • Rate Law Relationship: Rate = k [A] m [B] n

  2. Temperature

    • Higher temperature → Faster reaction (molecules have more energy).
    • More molecules exceed the activation energy (Eₐ) required for reaction.
    • Follows the Arrhenius equation: k = A e - E a R T
    • Example: Food spoils faster at room temperature than in the refrigerator.

  3. Pressure (for Gaseous Reactions)

    • Higher pressure → Faster reaction (molecules are closer, increasing collisions).
    • Applies mainly to gaseous reactions.
    • Example: In the Haber process, increasing pressure speeds up ammonia ( NH 3 ) formation.

  4. Surface Area of Reactants

    • More surface area → Faster reaction (more reactant particles exposed).
    • Example: Finely powdered sugar burns faster than a sugar cube.

  5. Catalyst

    • Lowers activation energy and provides an alternative reaction pathway.
    • Does not get consumed in the reaction.
    • Example: Enzymes in digestion speed up biological reactions.

  6. Nature of Reactants

    • Ionic reactions → Fast (no bond breaking needed).
    • Covalent reactions → Slow (bond breaking required).
    • Example: NaCl dissolves quickly in water, while sugar takes longer.
Chapter 5 - Chemical Equilibrium

1. Equilibrium Constant (K)

Definition

  • The Equilibrium Constant (K) expresses the ratio of product concentrations to reactant concentrations at equilibrium.
  • Formula: K = [ C ] c [ D ] d [ A ] a [ B ] b

Types of Equilibrium Constants

  1. Kc (Concentration-Based)

    • Uses molar concentrations (mol/L).
    • Example: N 2 + 3 H 2 ⇌ 2 NH 3

  2. Kp (Pressure-Based)

    • Used for gaseous reactions, based on partial pressures.
    • Related to Kc by: K p = K c ( R T ) Δn

  3. Ksp (Solubility Product Constant)

    • Used for sparingly soluble salts.
    • Example: CaF 2 ⇌ Ca ^ 2+ + 2 F ^ -

Relationship Between Gibbs Free Energy (ΔG) and K

  • Formula: ΔG = - R T ln K
  • If ΔG < 0 → Spontaneous reaction (K > 1).
  • If ΔG > 0 → Non-spontaneous reaction (K < 1).

Effect of Temperature on K

  • Endothermic Reactions: Increasing temperature increases K.
  • Exothermic Reactions: Increasing temperature decreases K.

2. Le Chatelier’s Principle

Introduction

  • Le Chatelier’s Principle states that if a system at equilibrium is disturbed by an external change (like concentration, temperature, or pressure), the system will adjust itself to counteract the disturbance and restore equilibrium.

Factors Affecting Equilibrium

i. Change in Concentration

  • Adding reactants → Shifts equilibrium right (toward products).
  • Adding products → Shifts equilibrium left (toward reactants).
  • Removing reactants → Shifts equilibrium left.
  • Removing products → Shifts equilibrium right.

Example: \( N_2 + 3H_2 \rightleftharpoons 2NH_3 \)

  • Adding H₂ or N₂ → More NH₃ forms.
  • Removing NH₃ → Equilibrium shifts right.

ii. Change in Pressure (Only for Gases)

  • Increasing pressure → Shifts equilibrium to the side with fewer gas molecules.
  • Decreasing pressure → Shifts equilibrium to the side with more gas molecules.
  • If moles of gas are the same on both sides, pressure change has no effect.

Example: \( N_2 + 3H_2 \rightleftharpoons 2NH_3 \)

  • 4 reactant gas molecules → 2 product gas molecules.
  • Increasing pressure → Shifts equilibrium right (towards fewer molecules).

iii. Change in Temperature

  • Exothermic reaction (ΔH<0) → Heat is a product.
    • Increasing temperature → Shifts equilibrium left (more reactants).
    • Decreasing temperature → Shifts equilibrium right (more products).
  • Endothermic reaction (ΔH>0) → Heat is a reactant.
    • Increasing temperature → Shifts equilibrium right (more products).
    • Decreasing temperature → Shifts equilibrium left (more reactants).

Example: \( N_2 + 3H_2 \rightleftharpoons 2NH_3 + \text{heat} \)

  • Increasing temperature → Shifts left, reducing NH₃ production.
  • Decreasing temperature → Shifts right, increasing NH₃ production.

iv. Effect of Catalyst

  • Catalysts do not shift equilibrium; they only help the system reach equilibrium faster by lowering activation energy.

3. Acid-Base Equilibrium

Introduction

  • Acid-base equilibrium deals with the balance between acids and bases in a solution.
  • It is governed by the dissociation of acids and bases and their respective equilibrium constants.

Acid Dissociation Constant (Ka)

  • Measures the strength of an acid.
  • Formula: K a = [H+][A-] [HA]
  • Larger Ka → Stronger acid (more dissociation).
  • Smaller Ka → Weaker acid (less dissociation).

Base Dissociation Constant (Kb)

  • Measures the strength of a base.
  • Formula: K b = [BH+][OH-] [B]
  • Larger Kb → Stronger base.
  • Smaller Kb → Weaker base.

Relationship Between Ka and Kb

  • For a conjugate acid-base pair:
  • Formula: Ka × Kb = Kw
  • Kw = 1.0 × 10-14 at 25°C
  • pKa + pKb = 14

pH and pOH

  • pH Calculation: pH = -log[H+]
  • pOH Calculation: pOH = -log[OH-]
  • pH + pOH = 14
  • Acidic Solution: pH < 7
  • Neutral Solution: pH = 7
  • Basic Solution: pH > 7

Buffer Solutions

  • A buffer resists changes in pH when small amounts of acid or base are added.
  • Henderson-Hasselbalch Equation:
  • Formula: pH = pKa + log([A-]/[HA])
Chapter 6 - Electrochemistry

1. Redox Reactions (Oxidation-Reduction Reactions)

Introduction

  • Redox reactions involve the simultaneous oxidation (loss of electrons) and reduction (gain of electrons).
  • These reactions are crucial in chemical, biological, and industrial processes.

Oxidation and Reduction

  1. Oxidation

    • Loss of electrons.
    • Increase in oxidation number.
    • Addition of oxygen or removal of hydrogen.
    • Example: Zn → Zn2+ + 2e- (Zinc is oxidized.)

  2. Reduction

    • Gain of electrons.
    • Decrease in oxidation number.
    • Removal of oxygen or addition of hydrogen.
    • Example: Cu2+ + 2e- → Cu (Copper ion is reduced.)

Mnemonic to Remember

  • OIL RIG:
  • Oxidation Is Loss (of electrons).
  • Reduction Is Gain (of electrons).

Oxidizing and Reducing Agents

Term Definition Example
Oxidizing Agent Gains electrons and gets reduced. MnO4- in acidic medium
Reducing Agent Loses electrons and gets oxidized. Zn metal

Oxidation Number Rules

  • Free elements: Oxidation number = 0 (e.g., O2, N2).
  • Monoatomic ions: Oxidation number = charge of ion (e.g., Na+ = +1).
  • Oxygen = -2 (except in peroxides, where it is -1).
  • Hydrogen = +1 (except in metal hydrides, where it is -1).
  • Sum of oxidation numbers in a neutral compound = 0.

Balancing Redox Reactions

Half-Reaction Method (Acidic Medium)

  1. Split reaction into oxidation and reduction half-reactions.
  2. Balance all elements except H and O.
  3. Balance oxygen with H2O.
  4. Balance hydrogen with H+.
  5. Balance charge using electrons.
  6. Multiply half-reactions to cancel electrons and combine.

Example: MnO4- + Fe2+ → Mn2+ + Fe3+ (in acidic medium)

Types of Redox Reactions

Type Description Example
Combination Two reactants form one product. 2Mg + O2 → 2MgO
Decomposition One compound breaks into multiple products. 2H2O → 2H2 + O2
Displacement One element replaces another in a compound. Zn + CuSO4 → ZnSO4 + Cu

Redox in Everyday Life

  • Corrosion: Rusting of iron is a redox reaction.
  • Electrochemical Cells: Batteries work on redox reactions.
  • Respiration: Oxidation of glucose releases energy.

2. Electrochemical Cells

Introduction

  • Electrochemical cells convert chemical energy into electrical energy (or vice versa) through redox reactions.
  • Used in batteries, corrosion protection, and electrolysis.

Types of Electrochemical Cells

  • Galvanic (Voltaic) Cells – Convert chemical energy into electrical energy.
  • Electrolytic Cells – Use electrical energy to drive a chemical reaction.

Galvanic (Voltaic) Cells

Definition

A Galvanic cell generates electrical energy from a spontaneous redox reaction.

Components

  • Anode (-) – Where oxidation occurs (electrons are lost).
  • Cathode (+) – Where reduction occurs (electrons are gained).
  • Electrolyte – Solution containing ions that facilitate the reaction.
  • Salt Bridge – Maintains charge balance by allowing ion exchange.
  • External Circuit – Allows electron flow from anode to cathode.

Example: Daniel Cell (Zn-Cu Cell)

  • Oxidation at Anode: Zn(s) → Zn²⁺(aq) + 2e⁻
  • Reduction at Cathode: Cu²⁺(aq) + 2e⁻ → Cu(s)
  • Electrons flow from Zn (anode) to Cu (cathode) through the wire.
  • Voltage generated: ~1.1V.

Electrolytic Cells

Definition

An electrolytic cell uses an external power source to drive a non-spontaneous redox reaction.

Components

  • Anode (+) – Oxidation occurs (electrons are lost).
  • Cathode (-) – Reduction occurs (electrons are gained).
  • Electrolyte – Conducts ions.
  • Battery/Power Source – Provides energy.

Example: Electrolysis of Water

  • 2H₂O(l) → 2H₂(g) + O₂(g)
  • H₂ forms at Cathode (-), O₂ forms at Anode (+).

Differences Between Galvanic & Electrolytic Cells

Feature Galvanic Cell Electrolytic Cell
Energy Conversion Chemical → Electrical Electrical → Chemical
Reaction Type Spontaneous Non-Spontaneous
Anode Charge Negative (-) Positive (+)
Cathode Charge Positive (+) Negative (-)

Standard Electrode Potential (E°)

  • Each half-cell has a standard reduction potential (E°) measured in volts.
  • Cell potential: E°cell = E°cathode - E°anode
  • If E°cell > 0, the reaction is spontaneous (Galvanic Cell).
  • If E°cell < 0, the reaction is non-spontaneous (Electrolytic Cell).

Example Calculation (Zn-Cu Cell)

  • E°(Cu²⁺/Cu) = +0.34V
  • E°(Zn²⁺/Zn) = -0.76V
  • E°cell = (0.34) - (-0.76) = 1.10V
  • Since E°cell > 0, the reaction is spontaneous.

Applications of Electrochemical Cells

  • Batteries (Alkaline, Lithium-ion, Lead-acid).
  • Corrosion Prevention (Galvanization, Cathodic protection).
  • Electroplating (Gold/Silver plating on jewelry).
  • Industrial Electrolysis (Chlorine, Hydrogen production).

Nernst Equation

Introduction

  • The Nernst equation calculates the cell potential (E) of an electrochemical cell under non-standard conditions.
  • It accounts for the effect of ion concentration on cell voltage.

Standard Cell Potential

  • Formula: \( E_{cell}^o = E_{cathode}^o - E_{anode}^o \)
  • Applies only at standard conditions:
    • Temperature: 298 K
    • Pressure: 1 atm
    • Concentration: 1 M

Nernst Equation Formula

  • At 298 K: \( E = E^o - \frac{0.0591}{n} \log Q \)
  • General form: \( E = E^o - \frac{RT}{nF} \ln Q \)
  • Where:
    • R = Gas constant (8.314 J/mol·K)
    • T = Temperature in Kelvin
    • n = Number of electrons transferred
    • F = Faraday’s constant (96485 C/mol)
    • Q = Reaction quotient

Reaction Quotient (Q)

Formula: \( Q = \frac{[Products]^{coefficients}}{[Reactants]^{coefficients}} \)

Example Calculation

Zn-Cu Galvanic Cell:

  • Given:
    • \( E^o(Zn^{2+}/Zn) = -0.76V \)
    • \( E^o(Cu^{2+}/Cu) = +0.34V \)
    • Concentrations: [Cu²⁺] = 0.01M, [Zn²⁺] = 1M
    • n = 2 electrons
  • Step 1: \( E_{cell}^o = 0.34V - (-0.76V) = 1.10V
  • Step 2: \( Q = \frac{[Zn^{2+}]}{[Cu^{2+}]} = \frac{1}{0.01} = 100 \)
  • Step 3: \( E = 1.10V - \frac{0.0591}{2} \log (100) \)
  • Final Answer: \( E = 1.04V \)

Special Cases

  • Effect of Concentration on E:
    • Increasing reactant concentration increases E.
    • Increasing product concentration decreases E.
  • Equilibrium Condition: When E = 0, \( Q = K_{eq} \), and:
    • \( \log K_{eq} = \frac{nE^o}{0.0591} \)
  • Concentration Cells:
    • Operate due to concentration differences.
    • Formula: \( E = -\frac{0.0591}{n} \log \frac{[dilute]}{[concentrated]} \)

Applications of the Nernst Equation

  • Batteries – Predicting battery voltage at different charge levels.
  • Biological Systems – Ion movement in neurons.
  • Corrosion Prevention – Understanding metal reactions.
  • Industrial Electrolysis – Controlling electrochemical processes.

2. Colligative Properties

Introduction

Colligative properties are physical properties of solutions that depend only on the number of solute particles, not on their identity.

Key Colligative Properties:

  • Relative Lowering of Vapor Pressure
  • Boiling Point Elevation
  • Freezing Point Depression
  • Osmotic Pressure

Relative Lowering of Vapor Pressure

When a non-volatile solute is added to a solvent, the vapor pressure of the solution decreases.

Raoult’s Law:

P solution = X solvent P solvent ^o

Where:

  • P solution = Vapor pressure of solution
  • P solvent ^o = Vapor pressure of pure solvent
  • X solvent = Mole fraction of solvent

Boiling Point Elevation

Adding a solute increases the boiling point of a solvent.

ΔT b = i K b m

Where:

  • ΔT b = Boiling point elevation
  • i = Van’t Hoff factor
  • K b = Boiling point elevation constant
  • m = Molality of solution

Freezing Point Depression

Adding a solute lowers the freezing point of a solvent.

ΔT f = i K f m

Where:

  • ΔT f = Freezing point depression
  • K f = Freezing point depression constant
  • i = Van’t Hoff factor
  • m = Molality of solution

Osmotic Pressure

Osmotic pressure is the pressure required to stop the flow of solvent through a semi-permeable membrane.

π = i M R T

Where:

  • π = Osmotic pressure
  • M = Molarity of solution
  • R = Gas constant (0.0821 L·atm/mol·K)
  • T = Temperature in Kelvin
  • i = Van’t Hoff factor

Van’t Hoff Factor

The Van’t Hoff factor accounts for ionization of solutes.

  • For non-electrolytes: i = 1 (e.g., glucose, urea).
  • For ionic compounds:
    • NaCl → i = 2 (Na⁺ + Cl⁻)
    • CaCl₂ → i = 3 (Ca²⁺ + 2Cl⁻)

Chapter 8 - Periodic Properties

1. Periodic Trends

Introduction

Periodic trends are patterns in properties of elements across the periodic table, influenced by atomic structure and electron configuration. These trends help predict element behavior and are crucial in chemistry, materials science, and engineering.

Key Periodic Trends

1. Atomic Radius

Definition: The distance from the nucleus to the outermost electron.

  • Decreases across a period (→): Due to increasing nuclear charge pulling electrons closer.
  • Increases down a group (↓): Due to additional electron shells increasing size.
  • Example: Li > Be > B > C (Atomic size decreases across Period 2).

2. Ionic Radius

Definition: The size of an ion compared to its neutral atom.

  • Cations (+) are smaller than their neutral atoms (fewer electrons, stronger nuclear pull).
  • Anions (−) are larger than their neutral atoms (more electrons, weaker pull).
  • Ionic size increases down a group (↓).
  • Example: Na⁺ < Na, Cl⁻> Cl.

3. Ionization Energy (IE)

Definition: Energy required to remove an electron from an atom.

  • Increases across a period (→): Due to increasing nuclear charge.
  • Decreases down a group (↓): Due to increased atomic size and shielding effect.
  • Example: Na (low IE) < Mg < Al < Cl (high IE).

4. Electron Affinity (EA)

Definition: Energy change when an atom gains an electron.

  • Increases (more negative) across a period (→): Stronger attraction for electrons.
  • Decreases (less negative) down a group (↓): Weaker attraction due to larger atomic size.
  • Example: Cl has higher EA than Na (halogens love gaining electrons).

5. Electronegativity

Definition: The ability of an atom to attract electrons in a bond.

  • Increases across a period (→): More protons = stronger electron pull.
  • Decreases down a group (↓): Larger atoms = weaker pull.
  • Example: F (highest electronegativity) > O > N > C > H.

6. Metallic & Non-Metallic Character

  • Metallic character decreases across a period (→) and increases down a group (↓).
  • Non-metallic character increases across a period (→) and decreases down a group (↓).
  • Example: Na (metal) < Si (semi-metal) < Cl (non-metal).

2.Atomic & Ionic Radii

Atomic Radius

Definition:

The atomic radius is the distance from the nucleus to the outermost electron in a neutral atom. It gives an idea of the size of an atom.

Trends in Atomic Radius:

  • Across a Period (→): Decreases
    • Due to increasing nuclear charge (more protons pull electrons closer).
    • No additional electron shells are added, so the atom contracts.
    • Example: Li > Be > B > C > N > O > F
  • Down a Group (↓): Increases
    • Due to the addition of electron shells, increasing atomic size.
    • Shielding effect reduces the pull of the nucleus on outer electrons.
    • Example: Li < Na < K < Rb < Cs

Comparison of Atomic Radii (in pm):

Element Li Be B C N O F
Radius (pm) 152 112 85 77 75 73 72

Ionic Radius

Definition:

The ionic radius is the radius of an ion after an atom gains or loses electrons.

Trends in Ionic Radius:

  • Cations (+) are smaller than their neutral atoms.
    • Losing electrons reduces electron-electron repulsion, allowing the nucleus to pull remaining electrons closer.
    • Example: Na (186 pm) > Na⁺ (102 pm)
  • Anions (−) are larger than their neutral atoms.
    • Gaining electrons increases repulsion, expanding the ion.
    • Example: Cl (99 pm) < Cl⁻ (181 pm)
  • Down a Group (↓): Ionic radius increases, like atomic radius.

Radius (pm) 102 72 54 181 184 140

3. Ionization Energy & Electron Affinity

i. Ionization Energy (IE)

Definition:

Ionization Energy is the energy required to remove an electron from a neutral atom in its gaseous state.

Equation:

X (g) + IE → X+ (g) + e-

Trends in Ionization Energy:

  • Across a Period (→): Increases
    • Reason: Increasing nuclear charge pulls electrons more tightly, making them harder to remove.
    • Example: Li < Be < B < C < N < O < F
  • Down a Group (↓): Decreases
    • Reason: Electrons are farther from the nucleus (more shielding), so they are easier to remove.
    • Example: Li > Na > K > Rb > Cs

Successive Ionization Energies:

  • Removing each successive electron requires more energy (IE₂ > IE₁).
  • Large jumps occur when removing core electrons.
  • Example:
    • IE₁ (Mg) = 738 kJ/mol
    • IE₂ (Mg) = 1451 kJ/mol
    • Removing the 3rd electron from Mg (forming Mg³⁺) requires a huge jump due to noble gas core stability.

ii. Electron Affinity (EA)

Definition:

Electron Affinity is the energy change when an atom gains an electron in its gaseous state.

Equation:

X (g) + e- → X- (g) + EA

Trends in Electron Affinity:

  • Across a Period (→): Becomes more negative (increases)
    • Reason: Higher nuclear charge attracts added electrons more strongly.
    • Example: N < O < F (most negative EA)
  • Down a Group (↓): Becomes less negative (decreases)
    • Reason: Larger atomic size weakens attraction for added electrons.
    • Example: Cl has a more negative EA than Br or I.

Highest & Lowest EA Elements:

  • Most negative EA: Chlorine (Cl)
  • Lowest EA: Noble gases (He, Ne, Ar), Be, Mg (full orbitals resist electron addition).
Chapter 9 - Chemical Bonding & Molecular Structure

1. Ionic, Covalent, and Metallic Bonding

Introduction

Chemical bonding occurs when atoms combine to form molecules or compounds. The three main types of bonds are:

  • Ionic Bonding: Transfer of electrons
  • Covalent Bonding: Sharing of electrons
  • Metallic Bonding: Sea of electrons

Ionic Bonding

Definition: Ionic bonding occurs when electrons are transferred from one atom to another, forming oppositely charged ions that attract each other.

  • Occurs between: Metals & Non-metals
  • Example: NaCl (Sodium chloride)

How It Works:

  • Metal atoms (like Na, Mg) lose electrons, forming cations (+ve ions).
  • Non-metal atoms (like Cl, O) gain electrons, forming anions (–ve ions).
  • The opposite charges attract, forming a strong electrostatic force.

Properties of Ionic Compounds:

  • High melting & boiling points (strong ionic bonds)
  • Soluble in water (polar nature)
  • Conduct electricity when molten or dissolved (ions move freely)
  • Brittle (ions shift under force, breaking structure)

Example Reaction: Na + Cl → Na⁺ + Cl⁻

Covalent
Bonding

Definition: Covalent bonding occurs when atoms share electrons to achieve stability.

  • Occurs between: Non-metal & Non-metal
  • Example: H₂O (Water), CO₂ (Carbon dioxide)

How It Works:

  • Each atom shares valence electrons to complete its octet (8 electrons in outer shell).
  • This forms molecules with strong covalent bonds between atoms.

Types of Covalent Bonds:

  • Single Bond (−): H₂, Cl₂ (1 pair of electrons shared)
  • Double Bond (=): O₂, CO₂ (2 pairs shared)
  • Triple Bond (≡): N₂, C₂H₂ (3 pairs shared)

Properties of Covalent Compounds:

  • Low melting & boiling points (weak intermolecular forces)
  • Poor electrical conductivity (no free electrons/ions)
  • Soluble in non-polar solvents
  • Forms gases, liquids, or soft solids

Example Reaction: H + H → H₂

Metallic Bonding

Definition: Metallic bonding occurs when metal atoms share electrons in a "sea of electrons," allowing free movement of electrons.

  • Occurs in: Metals
  • Example: Cu (Copper), Fe (Iron), Al (Aluminum)

How It Works:

  • Metal atoms lose their valence electrons, forming a lattice of positive ions.
  • The delocalized electrons move freely, creating strong attraction between ions.

Properties of Metallic Compounds:

  • High melting & boiling points (strong bonds)
  • Excellent conductivity (free-moving electrons)
  • Malleable & ductile (layers slide easily)
  • Lustrous (shiny) (electrons reflect light)

Example Structure: Cu + e⁻ (sea of electrons)

2. Coordination Chemistry

Introduction to Coordination Chemistry

Coordination chemistry deals with the study of coordination compounds, which consist of a central metal atom/ion surrounded by ligands. These complexes play important roles in biological systems, catalysis, and industrial applications.

Key Terms & Definitions

Term Definition
Coordination Compound A compound containing a metal center bonded to ligands via coordinate bonds.
Central Metal Atom/Ion A metal (usually transition metal) that accepts electrons from ligands.
Ligand A molecule or ion that donates a pair of electrons to the metal center.
Coordination Number (CN) The number of ligand atoms directly bonded to the metal center.
Coordination Sphere The central metal and its directly attached ligands, enclosed in brackets [ ].
Chelation The formation of stable rings when a ligand binds to a metal at multiple sites.
Complex Ion A charged species consisting of a metal center and its ligands.
Counter Ion An ion outside the coordination sphere that balances the charge of the complex.

Types of Ligands

Type of Ligand Definition Examples
Monodentate Binds to metal through a single donor atom H₂O, NH₃, Cl⁻
Bidentate Binds through two donor atoms Ethylenediamine (en), C₂O₄²⁻ (oxalate)
Polydentate Binds through multiple donor atoms EDTA⁴⁻ (hexadentate ligand)
Ambidentate Can bind through different atoms NO₂⁻ (via N or O), SCN⁻ (via S or N)

Coordination Number & Geometries

Coordination Number (CN) Common Geometry Example
2 Linear [Ag(NH₃)₂]⁺
4 Tetrahedral [NiCl₄]²⁻
4 Square Planar [Pt(NH₃)₂Cl₂]
6 Octahedral [Fe(CN)₆]³⁻

Nomenclature of Coordination Compounds

  • Cation named first, then anion.
  • Ligands named in alphabetical order before metal.
  • Neutral ligands use their normal names (e.g., NH₃ = ammine, H₂O = aqua).
  • Anionic ligands end in "-o" (e.g., Cl⁻ = chloro, CN⁻ = cyano).
  • Metal oxidation state is written in Roman numerals.
  • For anionic complexes, metal names end in "-ate" (e.g., Ferrate for Fe).

Examples:

  • [Co(NH₃)₆]Cl₃ → Hexaamminecobalt(III) chloride
  • [PtCl₂(NH₃)₂] → Diamminedichloroplatinum(II)
  • K₄[Fe(CN)₆] → Potassium hexacyanoferrate(II)

Isomerism in Coordination Compounds

Structural Isomerism

  • Ionization Isomerism: Different ions in solution
    Example: [Co(NH₃)₅Br]SO₄ vs [Co(NH₃)₅SO₄]Br
  • Coordination Isomerism: Ligands exchanged between cationic & anionic parts
    Example: [Cr(NH₃)₆][Co(CN)₆] vs [Co(NH₃)₆][Cr(CN)₆]
  • Linkage Isomerism: Ligand binds through different donor atoms
    Example: [Co(NO₂)(NH₃)₅]²⁺ (Nitro) vs [Co(ONO)(NH₃)₅]²⁺ (Nitrito)

Stereoisomerism

  • Geometrical Isomerism: Different spatial arrangements (cis/trans)
    Example: [Pt(NH₃)₂Cl₂] (Cisplatin – anti-cancer drug)
  • Optical Isomerism: Non-superimposable mirror images (chiral complexes)
    Example: [Co(en)₃]³⁺

Crystal Field Theory (CFT)

  • Explains the splitting of d-orbitals in a coordination complex due to ligand interaction.
  • Octahedral Complex: d-orbitals split into t₂g (low energy) and e₉ (high energy).
  • Tetrahedral Complex: Reverse splitting occurs (e and t₂ levels).
  • High Spin vs Low Spin Complexes:
    • Strong field ligands (CN⁻, CO) → Low spin (large splitting, electrons pair up).
    • Weak field ligands (Cl⁻, H₂O) → High spin (small splitting, electrons remain unpaired).

Applications of Coordination Compounds

  • Biological Systems: Hemoglobin (Fe complex), Vitamin B12 (Co complex).
  • Catalysis: Zeigler-Natta catalyst (TiCl₄ + AlR₃) for polymerization.
  • Medicinal Chemistry: Cisplatin (anti-cancer drug).
  • Extraction of Metals: Cyanide process for gold extraction.

1. Pollution & Green Chemistry

Pollution

Pollution is the contamination of the environment due to harmful substances, affecting air, water, and soil quality, leading to health hazards and ecosystem damage.

Types of Pollution

  • Air Pollution
    • Caused by gases (CO₂, SO₂, NO₂), particulate matter, and toxic chemicals.
    • Sources: Vehicle emissions, industries, burning fossil fuels.
    • Effects: Respiratory diseases, global warming, acid rain.
  • Water Pollution
    • Contamination of lakes, rivers, and oceans by harmful chemicals and waste.
    • Sources: Industrial waste, sewage, pesticides, oil spills.
    • Effects: Waterborne diseases, destruction of marine life.
  • Soil Pollution
    • Degradation of land due to harmful chemicals and waste disposal.
    • Sources: Pesticides, industrial waste, deforestation.
    • Effects: Loss of soil fertility, food contamination.
  • Noise Pollution
    • Harmful levels of sound from vehicles, machinery, and urbanization.
    • Effects: Stress, hearing loss, sleep disturbances.
  • Thermal Pollution
    • Increase in water temperature due to industrial discharge.
    • Effects: Disturbance in aquatic life.
  • Radioactive Pollution
    • Harmful radiation from nuclear plants, medical waste, and accidents.
    • Effects: Genetic mutations, cancer.

Green Chemistry

Green Chemistry focuses on designing chemical products and processes that minimize environmental impact and reduce pollution.

Principles of Green Chemistry

  • Prevent Waste: Minimize waste production instead of treating it later.
  • Atom Economy: Maximize the use of all materials in a reaction.
  • Less Hazardous Synthesis: Use safe and non-toxic chemicals.
  • Design Safer Chemicals: Reduce toxicity while maintaining function.
  • Use Safer Solvents & Auxiliaries: Prefer water or ethanol over harmful solvents.
  • Increase Energy Efficiency: Use catalysts, lower temperatures, and renewable energy.
  • Use Renewable Feedstocks: Replace petroleum-based chemicals with bio-based sources.
  • Reduce Derivatives: Avoid unnecessary modifications that generate waste.
  • Catalysis: Use catalysts instead of stoichiometric reagents to increase efficiency.
  • Biodegradable Products: Design products that break down naturally.
  • Monitor in Real-Time: Develop methods to track and prevent hazardous reactions.
  • Minimize Accidents: Reduce explosion, fire, and toxicity risks.

Applications of Green Chemistry

  • Biodegradable plastics (PLA, PHA) instead of traditional plastics.
  • Eco-friendly solvents like supercritical CO₂ in extractions.
  • Green catalysts for industrial reactions (enzymes, metal catalysts).
  • Water-based paints and coatings instead of toxic organic solvents.
  • Renewable biofuels to replace fossil fuels.
  • CO₂ capture and utilization to reduce emissions.

1. Nomenclature of Chemical Compounds

Definition

  • Nomenclature is the systematic way of naming chemical compounds based on their composition and structure. The IUPAC (International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry) provides standardized naming rules.

Nomenclature of Ionic Compounds

  • Ionic compounds consist of a metal (cation) and a non-metal (anion).

    • Rules for Naming Ionic Compounds

    • Name the metal (cation) first, followed by the non-metal (anion).
    • Change the anion's suffix to "-ide".
    • Example: NaCl → Sodium chloride
    • Example: MgO → Magnesium oxide
    • For transition metals, indicate oxidation state using Roman numerals.
    • Example: FeCl₂ → Iron(II) chloride
    • Example: FeCl₃ → Iron(III) chloride

Nomenclature of Covalent (Molecular) Compounds

  • Covalent compounds consist of two or more non-metals.

    • Rules for Naming Covalent Compounds

    • Use Greek prefixes to indicate the number of atoms.
    • 1 = Mono-, 2 = Di-, 3 = Tri-, 4 = Tetra-, 5 = Penta-, etc.
    • The second element’s name ends in "-ide".
    • Example: CO₂ → Carbon dioxide
    • Example: N₂O₅ → Dinitrogen pentoxide
    • If the first element has only one atom, omit "mono-".
    • Example: CO → Carbon monoxide, NOT Monocarbon monoxide

Nomenclature of Acids

  • Acids are named based on the anion they contain.

    • Acid Naming Rules

    • -ide (e.g., Cl⁻ = Chloride) → Hydro-___-ic acid (e.g., HCl = Hydrochloric acid)
    • -ate (e.g., NO₃⁻ = Nitrate) → ___-ic acid (e.g., HNO₃ = Nitric acid)
    • -ite (e.g., NO₂⁻ = Nitrite) → ___-ous acid (e.g., HNO₂ = Nitrous acid)

Nomenclature of Bases

  • Bases are named like ionic compounds.
  • Example: NaOH → Sodium hydroxide
  • Example: NH₄OH → Ammonium hydroxide

Nomenclature of Organic Compounds (Basic IUPAC Rules)

  • Organic compounds contain carbon and hydrogen (C-H bonds).

    • Alkanes (Single Bonds, -ane)

    • Formula: CₙH₂ₙ

    Alkynes (Triple Bonds, -yne)

    • Formula: CₙH₂ₙ₋₂
    • Example: C₂H₂ → Ethyne

2. Isomerism

Definition

  • Isomerism refers to the phenomenon where compounds have the same molecular formula but different structures or arrangements of atoms.
  • Isomers are compounds that exhibit this property.

Types of Isomerism

  • Isomerism is broadly classified into Structural Isomerism and Stereoisomerism.

i. Structural Isomerism

  • Structural isomers have different connectivity of atoms.

    • Types of Structural Isomerism

    1. Chain Isomerism (Skeletal Isomerism)

      • Different arrangements of the carbon chain.
      • Example: C₅H₁₂
        • n-Pentane (straight chain)
        • Iso-Pentane (branched)
        • Neo-Pentane (more branched)

    2. Position Isomerism

      • Functional group or substituent is placed at different positions.
      • Example: C₃H₇OH
        • Propan-1-ol (OH at C-1)
        • Propan-2-ol (OH at C-2)

    3. Functional Group Isomerism

      • Different functional groups but same molecular formula.
      • Example: C₂H₆O
        • Ethanol (Alcohol, -OH)
        • Dimethyl Ether (Ether, -O-)

    4. Metamerism

      • Different distribution of alkyl groups around a functional group.
      • Example: C₄H₁₀O (Ethers)
        • Ethoxyethane (C₂H₅-O-C₂H₅)
        • Methoxypropane (CH₃-O-C₃H₇)

    5. Tautomerism

      • Dynamic interconversion between isomers, usually involving proton migration.
      • Example: Keto-Enol Tautomerism
        • CH₃COCH₃ (Ketone form) ⇌ CH₂=CH-OH (Enol form)

ii. Stereoisomerism

  • Stereoisomers have the same connectivity but differ in spatial arrangement of atoms.

    • Types of Stereoisomerism

    1. Geometrical Isomerism (Cis-Trans or E-Z Isomerism)

      • Occurs in alkenes and cyclic compounds due to restricted rotation.
      • Example: But-2-ene (C₄H₈)
        • Cis-But-2-ene (Similar groups on the same side)
        • Trans-But-2-ene (Similar groups on opposite sides)

    2. Optical Isomerism

      • Molecules that can rotate plane-polarized light due to the presence of a chiral center (carbon with four different groups).
      • Example: Lactic Acid (C₃H₆O₃)
        • D-Lactic Acid (Rotates right, +)
        • L-Lactic Acid (Rotates left, -)

3. Electronic Effects in Organic Chemistry

Definition

  • Electronic effects influence the stability, reactivity, and properties of organic molecules. These effects arise due to the distribution of electrons in a molecule.

Types of Electronic Effects

  • The main types of electronic effects are: Inductive Effect (I Effect), Resonance Effect (Mesomeric Effect, R Effect), Hyperconjugation, and Electromeric Effect.

i. Inductive Effect (I Effect)

  • The inductive effect is the permanent shifting of electrons along a sigma (σ) bond due to the electronegativity difference between atoms.
  • Trend: Decreases as distance increases from the group causing the effect.

Types of Inductive Effect

  • (-I Effect) → Electron-withdrawing groups (reduce electron density).
    • Examples: NO₂, CN, COOH, F, Cl, Br, I
  • (+I Effect) → Electron-donating groups (increase electron density).
    • Examples: Alkyl groups (CH₃, C₂H₅), -O⁻, -NH₂

Example:

  • CH₃-CH₂-CH₂-Cl → Cl pulls electrons towards itself (-I effect).

ii. Resonance Effect (R Effect) / Mesomeric Effect

  • The resonance effect occurs when π-electrons delocalize in a conjugated system.
  • Resonance Structures: Different representations of the same molecule showing delocalization.
  • More resonance = More stability.

Types of Resonance Effect

  • (-R Effect) → Electron-withdrawing groups reduce electron density in benzene.
    • Examples: NO₂, CN, COOH, CHO, CO
  • (+R Effect) → Electron-donating groups increase electron density.
    • Examples: OH, NH₂, OCH₃, Cl, Br

Example:

  • Benzene with -OH → Activates the ring due to the +R Effect.

iii. Hyperconjugation ("No Bond Resonance")

  • Hyperconjugation occurs when sigma (σ) electrons delocalize into an adjacent empty or partially filled p-orbital.
  • Increases stability of carbocations and alkenes.
  • More alkyl groups = More hyperconjugation = More stability

Example:

  • (CH₃)₃C⁺ (Tertiary Carbocation) is more stable than CH₃CH₂⁺ (Primary Carbocation) due to hyperconjugation.

iv. Electromeric Effect (E Effect)

  • The electromeric effect is a temporary shift of electrons due to an attacking reagent.

Types of Electromeric Effect

  • (-E Effect) → Electron-withdrawing group pulls electrons towards itself.
  • (+E Effect) → Electron-donating group pushes electrons away.

Example:

  • CH₂=CH₂ + H⁺ → Electrons shift to one carbon to form a carbocation.

Definition

3. Aromatic Compounds

Definition

  • Aromatic compounds are cyclic, planar molecules with a conjugated π-electron system that follows Hückel’s Rule (4n + 2 π electrons). These compounds exhibit high stability due to resonance.

Examples:

  • Benzene (C₆H₆)
  • Toluene (C₆H₅CH₃)
  • Naphthalene (C₁₀H₈)
  • Phenol (C₆H₅OH)

Hückel’s Rule (4n + 2 Rule)

  • A compound is aromatic if it:
    • Is cyclic (ring structure).
    • Is planar (all atoms in the same plane).
    • Has conjugated π-electrons (alternating single and double bonds).
    • Follows the (4n + 2) rule, where n = 0, 1, 2, 3…
  • Example: Benzene (C₆H₆) has 6 π-electrons (n = 1, 4(1) + 2 = 6), so it is aromatic.

Types of Aromatic Compounds

Benzenoid Aromatic Compounds

  • Contain a benzene ring.
  • Examples: Benzene, Toluene, Phenol, Naphthalene.

Non-Benzenoid Aromatic Compounds

  • Do not contain a benzene ring but still follow Hückel’s Rule.
  • Examples: Cyclopropenyl cation (C₃H₃⁺), Tropolone.

Heterocyclic Aromatic Compounds

  • Contain at least one heteroatom (N, O, S) in the ring.
  • Examples:
    • Pyridine (C₅H₅N) → Contains Nitrogen (N).
    • Furan (C₄H₄O) → Contains Oxygen (O).
    • Thiophene (C₄H₄S) → Contains Sulfur (S).

Properties of Aromatic Compounds

  • Highly stable due to resonance.
  • Undergo substitution reactions instead of addition reactions.
  • Non-polar or slightly polar, insoluble in water but soluble in organic solvents.

Reactions of Aromatic Compounds

Electrophilic Substitution Reactions

  • Benzene reacts with electrophiles (E⁺) replacing a hydrogen atom.
  • Nitration: HNO₃ + H₂SO₄ → Nitrobenzene
    • C₆H₆ + HNO₃ → C₆H₅NO₂ + H₂O
  • Halogenation: Br₂ + FeBr₃ → Bromobenzene
    • C₆H₆ + Br₂ → C₆H₅Br + HBr
  • Friedel-Crafts Alkylation (Addition of R-group):
    • C₆H₆ + RCl → C₆H₅R + HCl (Catalyst: AlCl₃)
  • Friedel-Crafts Acylation (Addition of CO-R group):
    • C₆H₆ + RCOCl → C₆H₅COR + HCl
  • Sulfonation: SO₃ + H₂SO₄ → Benzene Sulfonic Acid
    • C₆H₆ + SO₃ → C₆H₅SO₃H

Oxidation Reactions

  • Alkyl groups attached to benzene undergo oxidation.
  • Toluene to Benzoic Acid:
    • C₆H₅CH₃ + [O] → C₆H₅COOH (Using KMnO₄)

Difference Between Aromatic & Aliphatic Compounds

Chapter 16 - Reaction Mechanisms

1. Substitution Reactions (SN1 & SN2)

Substitution reactions are reactions in which one atom or group in a molecule is replaced by another. These reactions are common in organic chemistry, especially for alkyl halides.

There are two main types of nucleophilic substitution reactions:

  • SN1 (Unimolecular Nucleophilic Substitution)
  • SN2 (Bimolecular Nucleophilic Substitution)

i. SN1 Reaction (Unimolecular Nucleophilic Substitution)

  • Mechanism: Two-step process
  • Rate Determining Step (RDS): Carbocation formation (slow step)
  • Rate Law: Rate = k [Alkyl Halide] (Depends only on substrate)

Steps of SN1 Reaction:

  1. Carbocation Formation (Slow Step): The leaving group (e.g., Cl⁻, Br⁻, I⁻) detaches, forming a carbocation.
    • Example: (CH₃)₃CBr → (CH₃)₃C⁺ + Br⁻
  2. Nucleophilic Attack (Fast Step): The nucleophile (e.g., H₂O, OH⁻) attacks the carbocation.
    • Example: (CH₃)₃C⁺ + H₂O → (CH₃)₃COH (Tert-Butanol)

Characteristics of SN1

  • Favored by: Tertiary (3°) alkyl halides (due to stable carbocation)
  • Solvent: Polar protic solvents (e.g., H₂O, alcohols)
  • Stereochemistry: Racemization (Mix of retention & inversion)
  • Reactivity Order: 3° > 2° > 1° (1° rarely undergoes SN1)

ii. SN2 Reaction (Bimolecular Nucleophilic Substitution)

  • Mechanism: One-step process (concerted reaction)
  • Rate Law: Rate = k [Alkyl Halide] [Nucleophile] (Depends on both)

Steps of SN2 Reaction:

  1. Backside Attack: The nucleophile attacks from the opposite side of the leaving group. Simultaneously, the leaving group departs.
    • Example: CH₃CH₂Br + OH⁻ → CH₃CH₂OH + Br⁻

Characteristics of SN2

  • Favored by: Primary (1°) alkyl halides
  • Solvent: Polar aprotic solvents (e.g., DMSO, acetone)
  • Stereochemistry: Inversion of configuration (Walden inversion)
  • Reactivity Order: 1° > 2° > 3° (3° does not undergo SN2)

Comparison: SN1 vs. SN2

Feature SN1 SN2
Rate Law Rate = k[RX] Rate = k[RX][Nu]
Mechanism Two-step (Carbocation intermediate) One-step (Concerted)
Nucleophile Weak (e.g., H₂O, ROH) Strong (e.g., OH⁻, CN⁻)
Substrate 3° > 2° > 1° 1° > 2° > 3°
Solvent Polar protic Polar aprotic
Stereochemistry Racemization Inversion (Walden)

Example Reactions

SN1 Example:

(CH₃)₃CBr + H₂O → (CH₃)₃COH + HBr (Tertiary butyl alcohol)

SN2 Example:

CH₃Br + OH⁻ → CH₃OH + Br⁻ (Methanol)

2. Addition & Elimination Reactions

Addition and elimination reactions are fundamental types of organic reactions. Addition reactions involve adding atoms or groups to a molecule, whereas elimination reactions involve the removal of atoms or groups, often forming multiple bonds.

Addition Reactions

Definition: Two reactants combine to form a single product.

Common in: Alkenes, Alkynes, Carbonyl compounds (Aldehydes, Ketones)

General Formula:

A = B + X − Y → A − X − B − Y

Types of Addition Reactions:

A. Electrophilic Addition (EA)

  • Occurs in: Alkenes & Alkynes
  • Mechanism: Electrophile (E⁺) attacks the π-bond, forming a carbocation. Nucleophile (Nu⁻) attacks the carbocation, leading to the final product.
Examples:
  • Hydrohalogenation (H-X addition): 
    CH₂=CH₂ + HBr → CH₃−CH₂Br
  • Hydration (H₂O addition in presence of acid catalyst H₃O⁺): 
    CH₂=CH₂ + H₂O → CH₃−CH₂OH
  • Halogenation (Br₂, Cl₂ addition): 
    CH₂=CH₂ + Br₂ → CH₂Br−CH₂Br

B. Nucleophilic Addition (NA)

  • Occurs in: Carbonyl Compounds (Aldehydes & Ketones)
  • Mechanism: Nucleophile (Nu⁻) attacks the electrophilic carbonyl (C=O). Oxygen gets a negative charge and then stabilizes.
Examples:
  • Addition of HCN to Aldehydes & Ketones: 
    CH₃CHO + HCN → CH₃CH(OH)CN

C. Free Radical Addition

  • Occurs in: Alkenes & Alkynes
  • Follows: Anti-Markovnikov’s Rule (Peroxide Effect)
Example:
  • HBr Addition in Presence of Peroxide: 
    CH₂=CH₂ + HBr → CH₃−CH₂Br

    *(Br⁻ adds first due to radicals)*

Elimination Reactions

Definition: A single reactant splits into two products, typically forming multiple bonds.

Common in: Alcohols, Alkyl Halides

General Formula:

                   A − X → A = B + X − Y

3. Rearrangement Reactions

Introduction

Rearrangement reactions are a class of organic reactions in which the structure of a molecule is rearranged to form a constitutional isomer. This usually involves the migration of atoms or functional groups within the molecule, leading to a more stable product.

General Characteristics of Rearrangement Reactions:

  • Involve the migration of atoms, groups, or bonds.
  • Often proceed through carbocation, radical, or carbanion intermediates.
  • Can be either intramolecular (within the same molecule) or intermolecular (between molecules).
  • Lead to the formation of more stable products based on electronic effects, steric hindrance, or conjugation.

Types of Rearrangement Reactions

Rearrangement reactions can be broadly classified into several types:

(i) Carbocation Rearrangements

These involve the migration of alkyl or aryl groups in a reaction involving a carbocation intermediate.

1. Wagner-Meerwein Rearrangement

  • A 1,2-alkyl shift that occurs in carbocations, generally in the presence of strong acids.

Example: Tertiary alcohol + HCl → Rearranged carbocation → More stable alkyl halide.

2. Pinacol-Pinacolone Rearrangement

  • Occurs when vicinal diols (glycols) are treated with acids.
  • One of the hydroxyl groups is protonated and lost as water, leading to a carbocation rearrangement.

Example: Pinacol → Pinacolone.

3. Demjanov Rearrangement

  • Involves the ring expansion of cyclic amines under acidic conditions.

Example: Cyclohexylamine + HNO₂ → Expanded ring structure.

4. Benzilic Acid Rearrangement

  • Involves the migration of a phenyl group in α-diketones under basic conditions.

Example: Benzil + Base → Benzilic acid.

(ii) Free Radical Rearrangements

These occur through radical intermediates.

1. Wohl-Ziegler Rearrangement

  • Involves the bromination of alkanes at the benzylic position using NBS (N-bromosuccinimide).

Example: Toluene + NBS → Benzyl bromide.

2. Hunsdiecker Reaction

  • Converts silver carboxylates into alkyl halides using bromine.

Example: Silver acetate + Br₂ → Ethyl bromide.

(iii) Anionic Rearrangements

These involve anionic intermediates.

1. Favorskii Rearrangement

  • Occurs when α-halo ketones react with strong bases, leading to ring contraction.

Example: Cyclopropanone formation from α-bromo ketone.

2. Benzoin Rearrangement

  • Occurs in the presence of cyanide ions, leading to α-hydroxy ketones.

Example: Benzaldehyde + KCN → Benzoin.

(iv) Pericyclic Rearrangements

These involve cyclic transition states and occur via concerted mechanisms.

1. Cope Rearrangement

  • A [3,3]-sigmatropic shift that occurs in 1,5-dienes.

Example: 1,5-hexadiene → Rearranged product.

2. Claisen Rearrangement

  • Involves the rearrangement of allyl aryl ethers to ortho-hydroxy aryl ketones.

Example: Allyl phenyl ether → Ortho-hydroxy aryl ketone.

(v) Aromatic Rearrangements

These involve rearrangements in aromatic systems.

1. Fries Rearrangement

  • Occurs when aryl esters react with aluminum chloride (AlCl₃), leading to hydroxyaryl ketones.

Example: Phenyl acetate + AlCl₃ → Hydroxyacetophenone.

2. Hofmann-Martius Rearrangement

  • Involves the rearrangement of aryl amines to ortho- and para-aminophenols.

Example: Aniline + Heat → o-Aminophenol and p-Aminophenol.

Mechanism of Rearrangement Reactions

The mechanism varies depending on the type of rearrangement but generally follows these steps:

  1. Formation of an Intermediate: The intermediate could be a carbocation, carbanion, radical, or cyclic transition state.
  2. Rearrangement Step: Migration of an atom or group to a different position to achieve a more stable intermediate.
  3. Product Formation: The rearranged intermediate stabilizes to give the final product.

Applications of Rearrangement Reactions

Rearrangement reactions are widely used in organic synthesis and industry.

Pharmaceutical Industry

  • Pinacol-Pinacolone rearrangement is used in the synthesis of steroids and hormones.
  • Hofmann rearrangement is used in the synthesis of amides and urea derivatives.

Polymer Industry

  • Fries rearrangement helps in the synthesis of phenolic resins.

Perfume and Flavor Industry

  • Claisen rearrangement is useful in producing essential oils.
Chapter 17 - Alcohols, Phenols, Ethers

1. Alcohols, Phenols, and Ethers

i. Alcohols (R-OH)

Organic compounds containing a hydroxyl (-OH) group attached to a saturated carbon.

Classification:

  • Primary (1°) Alcohol – OH attached to a carbon with one alkyl group (e.g., Ethanol).
  • Secondary (2°) Alcohol – OH attached to a carbon with two alkyl groups (e.g., Isopropanol).
  • Tertiary (3°) Alcohol – OH attached to a carbon with three alkyl groups (e.g., Tert-butanol).

Physical Properties:

  • Hydrogen bonding → High boiling points.
  • Soluble in water (solubility decreases with longer chains).

Chemical Reactions:

  • Oxidation:
    • 1° Alcohol → Aldehyde → Carboxylic acid
    • 2° Alcohol → Ketone
    • 3° Alcohol → No oxidation
  • Dehydration: Forms alkenes.
  • Reaction with Na: Produces hydrogen gas.

ii. Phenols (Ar-OH)

Aromatic compounds where the -OH group is directly attached to a benzene ring.

Physical Properties:

  • Higher boiling points due to hydrogen bonding.
  • Weakly acidic (forms phenoxide ion in water).

Chemical Reactions:

  • Acidic Nature: Reacts with NaOH but not with Na₂CO₃.
  • Electrophilic Substitution:
    • Bromination: Phenol + Br₂ → Tribromophenol
    • Nitration: Phenol + HNO₃ → Nitro phenol
  • Oxidation: Converts phenols into quinones.

iii. Ethers (R-O-R')

Compounds with an oxygen atom bonded to two alkyl or aryl groups.

Classification:

  • Symmetrical Ethers: Both groups are the same (e.g., Diethyl ether).
  • Asymmetrical Ethers: Different alkyl/aryl groups (e.g., Methyl phenyl ether).

Physical Properties:

  • Low boiling points (no hydrogen bonding).
  • Slightly soluble in water (forms H-bonds with water).

Chemical Reactions:

  • Cleavage by Acids: R-O-R + HI → Alkyl Halide + Alcohol
  • Auto-oxidation: Forms peroxides in air.
  • Williamson Ether Synthesis: R-X + R’-O⁻ → R-O-R’ (SN2 mechanism).

Applications:

  • Alcohols: Solvents, fuels, disinfectants.
  • Phenols: Antiseptics (e.g., Dettol), resins, dyes.
  • Ethers: Solvents (Diethyl ether as an anesthetic), fuel additives.

2. Aldehydes & Ketones

Introduction

Aldehydes and ketones are organic compounds containing the carbonyl group (-C=O).

i. Aldehydes (R-CHO)

The carbonyl group is attached to at least one hydrogen atom.

Examples:

  • Formaldehyde (HCHO)
  • Acetaldehyde (CH₃CHO)

Physical Properties:

  • Polar molecules, higher boiling points than alkanes but lower than alcohols.
  • Soluble in water (small aldehydes).

Chemical Reactions:

  • Oxidation: Aldehyde → Carboxylic acid (with KMnO₄, Tollens’ or Fehling’s test).
  • Reduction: Aldehyde → Primary alcohol (with LiAlH₄ or NaBH₄).
  • Addition Reactions:
    • H₂O addition → Forms geminal diols.
    • HCN addition → Forms cyanohydrins.
  • Aldol Condensation: Two aldehydes react in the presence of a base to form β-hydroxy aldehydes.

ii. Ketones (R-CO-R')

The carbonyl group is bonded to two alkyl or aryl groups.

Examples:

  • Acetone (CH₃COCH₃)
  • Butanone (CH₃COC₂H₅)

Physical Properties:

  • Similar to aldehydes but slightly less reactive.
  • Soluble in water (small ketones).

Chemical Reactions:

  • Reduction: Ketone → Secondary alcohol (with LiAlH₄ or NaBH₄).
  • Nucleophilic Addition: Reacts with HCN, NH₂OH, 2,4-DNP.
  • Aldol Condensation: Similar to aldehydes, ketones undergo self-condensation.
  • Iodoform Test: Methyl ketones react with I₂ + NaOH to give a yellow precipitate of CHI₃ (Iodoform test).

Applications

  • Aldehydes: Used in perfumes, dyes, and as preservatives (Formaldehyde).
  • Ketones: Used as solvents (Acetone), and in pharmaceuticals.

3. Carboxylic Acids and Derivatives

i. Carboxylic Acids (R-COOH)

Organic compounds containing the carboxyl (-COOH) group, which consists of a carbonyl (C=O) and hydroxyl (-OH) group.

Examples:

  • Formic Acid (HCOOH) – Found in ant venom.
  • Acetic Acid (CH₃COOH) – Found in vinegar.

Physical Properties:

  • High boiling points due to strong hydrogen bonding.
  • Soluble in water (small chain acids).
  • Weak acids (partially ionize in water).

Chemical Reactions:

  • Acidic Nature: Reacts with metals → H₂ gas. Reacts with bases → Forms salts.
  • Esterification: R-COOH + Alcohol → Ester + H₂O.
  • Decarboxylation: Carboxylic acid → Alkane (using soda lime).
  • Reduction: Carboxylic acid → Primary alcohol (with LiAlH₄).

ii. Carboxylic Acid Derivatives

Compounds derived from carboxylic acids by replacing the -OH group with another functional group.

Types & Reactions:

  • Esters (R-COOR’):
    • Sweet-smelling compounds used in perfumes.
    • Hydrolysis: Ester + H₂O → Carboxylic acid + Alcohol.
  • Acid Chlorides (R-COCl):
    • More reactive than carboxylic acids.
    • Reaction with Alcohols: R-COCl + R’OH → Ester + HCl.
  • Amides (R-CONH₂):
    • Formed by reaction of acid chloride with ammonia.
    • Hydrolysis: Amide + H₂O → Carboxylic acid + Ammonia.
  • Anhydrides (R-CO-O-CO-R’):
    • Formed by dehydration of carboxylic acids.
    • Reaction with Alcohols: Forms esters and acids.

Applications

  • Carboxylic Acids: Used in food, medicine, and industries (Acetic acid in vinegar).
  • Esters: Used in perfumes, flavors, and solvents.
  • Amides: Found in proteins and used in drug synthesis.
  • Acid Chlorides & Anhydrides: Used in organic synthesis.

4. Amines

Introduction

Amines are organic compounds derived from ammonia (NH₃) by replacing one or more hydrogen atoms with alkyl or aryl groups.

Classification of Amines

  • Primary (1°) Amine: One alkyl/aryl group attached to nitrogen (e.g., Methylamine, CH₃NH₂).
  • Secondary (2°) Amine: Two alkyl/aryl groups attached (e.g., Dimethylamine, (CH₃)₂NH).
  • Tertiary (3°) Amine: Three alkyl/aryl groups attached (e.g., Trimethylamine, (CH₃)₃N).
  • Quaternary Ammonium Salts: Four groups attached to nitrogen, forming a positively charged ion (e.g., Tetramethylammonium chloride, (CH₃)₄N⁺Cl⁻).

Physical Properties

  • Lower amines are gases, higher ones are liquids or solids.
  • Soluble in water due to hydrogen bonding (solubility decreases with higher molecular weight).
  • Fishy odor, commonly found in decaying organic matter.

Chemical Reactions

Basic Nature of Amines

Amines are weak bases (accept protons). They react with acids to form ammonium salts.

Example: CH₃NH₂ + HCl → CH₃NH₃⁺Cl⁻

Important Reactions

  • Acylation: R-NH₂ + R’-COCl → Amide + HCl.
  • Hofmann Bromamide Reaction: Amide + Br₂ + NaOH → Primary Amine (1°) + NaBr + CO₂.
  • Gabriel Phthalimide Synthesis: Used to prepare pure primary amines.
  • Reaction with Nitrous Acid (HNO₂):
    • 1° Amine → Alcohol + N₂ Gas.
    • 2° Amine → Nitrosoamine.
    • 3° Amine → No reaction.
  • Hinsberg Test: Differentiates 1°, 2°, and 3° amines using benzene sulfonyl chloride.

Aromatic Amines (Aniline, C₆H₅NH₂)

Stronger base than ammonia but weaker than aliphatic amines. Undergo electrophilic substitution (e.g., bromination, nitration).

Applications

  • Dyes & Pigments: Used in azo dyes.
  • Pharmaceuticals: Used in medicines (e.g., sulfa drugs).
  • Polymers: Used in nylon production.
  • Agriculture: Used in pesticides and fertilizers.
Chapter 17 - Biomolecules

1. Carbohydrates

Introduction

Carbohydrates are organic compounds made of carbon (C), hydrogen (H), and oxygen (O), usually in the ratio Cₙ(H₂O)ₙ. They are the primary source of energy in living organisms.

Classification of Carbohydrates

A. Monosaccharides (Simple Sugars)

Single sugar unit (cannot be hydrolyzed further).

  • Glucose (C₆H₁₂O₆): Main energy source.
  • Fructose: Found in fruits.
  • Galactose: Found in milk.

B. Disaccharides (Two Sugar Units)

Formed by the condensation of two monosaccharides.

  • Sucrose (Glucose + Fructose): Table sugar.
  • Lactose (Glucose + Galactose): Found in milk.
  • Maltose (Glucose + Glucose): Found in malt.

C. Polysaccharides (Complex Carbohydrates)

Long chains of monosaccharides linked together.

  • Starch: Energy storage in plants.
  • Glycogen: Energy storage in animals.
  • Cellulose: Structural component of plant cell walls.

Properties of Carbohydrates

  • Soluble in water (monosaccharides & disaccharides).
  • Sweet in taste (simple sugars).
  • Reducing & Non-Reducing Sugars:
    • Reducing sugars (Glucose, Lactose, Maltose) react with Fehling’s/Tollens’ reagent.
    • Non-reducing sugar (Sucrose) does not react.

Chemical Reactions

  • Hydrolysis: Polysaccharides → Disaccharides → Monosaccharides.
  • Fermentation: Glucose → Ethanol + CO₂ (by yeast).
  • Oxidation: Glucose oxidizes to form gluconic acid.

Biological Importance

  • Energy Source: Glucose provides ATP through respiration.
  • Structural Role: Cellulose in plants, chitin in arthropods.
  • Storage Form: Glycogen in animals, starch in plants.

2. Proteins

Introduction

Proteins are complex biomolecules made up of amino acids linked by peptide bonds. They are essential for body functions like growth, repair, and metabolism.

Structure of Proteins

Amino Acids:

  • Building blocks of proteins.
  • Contain amino (-NH₂) and carboxyl (-COOH) groups.
  • Essential amino acids (cannot be synthesized by the body) – e.g., Lysine, Valine.

Peptide Bond Formation:

Amino acids join via peptide bonds (-CO-NH-), forming polypeptides.

Levels of Protein Structure:

  • Primary Structure: Sequence of amino acids in a polypeptide chain.
  • Secondary Structure: α-Helix and β-Sheet due to hydrogen bonding.
  • Tertiary Structure: 3D folding due to interactions like disulfide bonds.
  • Quaternary Structure: Multiple polypeptide chains forming a complex protein (e.g., Hemoglobin).

Types of Proteins

Fibrous Proteins:

  • Long, strong fibers, insoluble in water.
  • Example: Collagen (skin), Keratin (hair, nails), Myosin (muscles).

Globular Proteins:

  • Spherical, water-soluble, functional proteins.
  • Example: Enzymes, Hemoglobin, Antibodies, Hormones (Insulin).

Properties of Proteins

  • Denaturation: Loss of structure due to heat, pH change (e.g., cooking eggs).
  • Solubility: Globular proteins are water-soluble, fibrous proteins are not.
  • Isoelectric Point (pI): pH at which the protein carries no net charge.

Functions of Proteins

  • Structural Role: Collagen (connective tissues).
  • Enzymes: Speed up biological reactions (e.g., Amylase, DNA Polymerase).
  • Hormones: Insulin regulates blood sugar.
  • Transport: Hemoglobin carries oxygen.
  • Immunity: Antibodies fight infections.

15. Nucleic Acids

Introduction

Nucleic acids are biomolecules that store and transmit genetic information. They are made up of nucleotides and play a crucial role in protein synthesis.

Types of Nucleic Acids

DNA (Deoxyribonucleic Acid)

  • Double-stranded helix (Watson & Crick model).
  • Stores genetic information in the nucleus.
  • Sugar: Deoxyribose.
  • Bases: Adenine (A), Thymine (T), Cytosine (C), Guanine (G).
  • Base pairing: A-T (2 hydrogen bonds), C-G (3 hydrogen bonds).

RNA (Ribonucleic Acid)

  • Single-stranded molecule.
  • Helps in protein synthesis.
  • Sugar: Ribose.
  • Bases: Adenine (A), Uracil (U), Cytosine (C), Guanine (G).
  • Base pairing: A-U, C-G.

Types of RNA:

  • mRNA (Messenger RNA): Carries genetic code.
  • tRNA (Transfer RNA): Transfers amino acids.
  • rRNA (Ribosomal RNA): Forms ribosomes.

Structure of Nucleotides

Each nucleotide consists of:

  • Nitrogenous Base: Purines (A, G); Pyrimidines (C, T/U).
  • Pentose Sugar: Ribose in RNA, Deoxyribose in DNA.
  • Phosphate Group: Forms backbone.

Nucleotides join via phosphodiester bonds to form nucleic acids.

Functions of Nucleic Acids

  • Genetic Information Storage: DNA.
  • Protein Synthesis: RNA.
  • Regulation of Cellular Activities.
  • Inheritance & Evolution.
